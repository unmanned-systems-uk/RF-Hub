ACT I

The Voltage Source Creates Current

Everything starts with your transmitter pushing electrons back and forth along the antenna wire. This isn't a one-way flow like DC current — it's alternating current (AC), reversing direction millions of times per second.

Diagram 1 · AC Current in a Dipole

Notice something crucial: current is strongest at the centre (feed point) and drops to zero at the tips. Meanwhile, voltage does the opposite — highest at the tips, lowest at the centre. This distribution pattern is fundamental to how the antenna radiates.

🪢

Think of a skipping rope

Shake one end of a skipping rope — the movement is biggest where you're holding it (like current at the feed point) and the rope barely moves at the far end where it's tied off (like the antenna tips where current is zero). The "standing wave" pattern on the rope is directly analogous to the current distribution on a dipole.

🤔 But wait — why doesn't current just flow straight through?

Because there's nowhere for it to go! The antenna tips are open-ended. The electrons rush towards the tip, pile up (creating high voltage), then get pushed back the other way. This back-and-forth creates a standing wave of current on the antenna — and it's this oscillating current that creates the electromagnetic fields.

ACT II

Fields Form Around the Wire

Here's where it gets beautiful. The oscillating current doesn't just stay in the wire — it creates two types of invisible field around it. These aren't two separate things; they're two inseparable aspects of the same phenomenon.

Diagram 2 · The Electric (E) Field
⚡
The E (Electric) field is created by the charge difference along the antenna. When current flows, electrons pile up at one end and leave a deficit at the other. This creates a voltage difference, and the E field lines point from the positive to the negative region. The E field oscillates in the plane of the antenna wire — for a vertical dipole, the E field is vertical. This is what we mean by vertical polarisation.
Diagram 3 · The Magnetic (H) Field
🧲
The H (Magnetic) field is created by the current itself — moving charges always create a magnetic field. The H field forms closed loops that wrap around the wire like rings on a finger. Use the right-hand rule: point your thumb in the direction of current flow, and your fingers curl in the direction of the H field. For a vertical dipole, the H field is horizontal.

🤔 Why do we say H field instead of B field?

Both describe magnetism, but they're subtly different. B is the magnetic flux density (measured in Tesla) — the "total" magnetic field including any effects of the material it passes through. H is the magnetic field intensity (measured in A/m) — it represents the "driving force" of the field, independent of the medium. In free space (air), they're directly proportional: B = μ₀ × H. In antenna work, we tend to use H because we're interested in the field produced by the current, in free space.

Diagram 4 · E and H Together — The Electromagnetic Field

This is the critical insight: the E and H fields are always perpendicular to each other, and both are perpendicular to the direction the wave travels. This isn't a coincidence — it's a fundamental requirement of Maxwell's equations. One field creates the other, and vice versa, in an endless self-sustaining dance.

🔑
This is why they can't be separated. A changing electric field creates a magnetic field. A changing magnetic field creates an electric field. Each one generates the other. Once launched from the antenna, this self-sustaining cycle carries the wave through space at the speed of light — no wire needed.
ACT III

The Fields Detach and Propagate

This is the part that makes most people's brains short-circuit, and it's the hardest step in the original diagram to understand. How do the fields "leave" the wire?

The answer is: they don't so much "leave" as they get left behind.

Diagram 5 · How Fields Detach — The Half-Cycle Story
🫧

Think of blowing soap bubbles

You blow through the ring (current flowing) and a bubble forms (field expands). When you pull back to take another breath (current reverses), the bubble pinches off from the ring and floats away on its own (self-sustaining EM wave). Each breath launches another bubble. The bubbles don't need the ring anymore once they've detached — and EM waves don't need the antenna once they've launched.

🤔 Why does the antenna length matter?

For the fields to detach efficiently, the antenna needs to be close to half a wavelength long. At this length, the current and voltage distributions create the maximum "push" for the fields to separate from the wire. Too short, and the fields form but collapse back onto the wire before they can detach — the energy goes back into the circuit as heat instead of radiation. This is why a short antenna needs a loading coil to compensate, and why SWR goes bad when the antenna isn't resonant.

Connecting to the radiation pattern

Now here's where your two concepts unite. The fields detach in all directions around the wire, but not equally. The dipole radiates most strongly broadside (perpendicular to the wire) and almost nothing off the tips. Why? Because off the tips, the current elements on opposite sides of the feed point produce fields that cancel each other out.

Diagram 6 · From Fields to Radiation Pattern

The radiation pattern is simply a map of "how much field strength arrives in each direction." It's the same E and H fields from Act II, but the pattern tells you where the energy goes. They're not separate things — the pattern is the spatial distribution of those fields.

ACT IV

Fields Induce Current in the Receiver

The beautiful symmetry of electromagnetic radiation: the entire process runs in reverse at the receiving end.

Diagram 7 · The Complete Link — TX to RX

The arriving E field pushes electrons back and forth in the receiving antenna, creating an alternating current — a tiny mirror of the original transmitter signal. The H field contributes too, inducing voltage through Faraday's law. The receiver amplifies this microscopic signal back to something useful.

📐
Polarisation matching matters. The receiving antenna must be oriented to align with the incoming E field. If the transmitter uses a vertical dipole (vertical E field), the receiver works best as a vertical dipole too. Turn the receiving antenna horizontal and the E field can't push electrons along it — you lose the signal. This is why polarisation is one of the first things you check when setting up a link.
SUMMARY

Bringing It All Together

To answer your original question: E and H field propagation and the radiation pattern are two views of the same phenomenon, not separate entities.

E Field

Created by charge distribution (voltage). Oscillates in the plane of the antenna. Defines the polarisation. Pushes electrons in the receiving antenna.

H Field

Created by current flow. Wraps around the wire, perpendicular to E. Always accompanies E — you cannot have one without the other in a propagating wave.

EM Wave

E and H together, self-sustaining. Each creates the other as they change. Travels at the speed of light. The "stuff" that carries your signal through space.

Radiation Pattern

A map showing WHERE the EM wave energy goes. Shaped by the antenna geometry. Not a separate thing — it's the spatial distribution of the E and H fields.

🎯 The one-sentence version

The E and H fields are WHAT propagates; the radiation pattern is WHERE it propagates.
