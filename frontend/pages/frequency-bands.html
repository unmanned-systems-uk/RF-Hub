Sub-Radio Frequencies (DC – 30 kHz)

FrequencyWavelengthApplicationsCharacteristics

FrequencyWavelengthApplicationsCharacteristics
300 Hz – 3 kHz1,000 km – 100 kmAudio frequencies, submarine comms, geophysicsPenetrates seawater, extremely long wavelengths

Notable: 50/60 Hz power lines (50 Hz in UK), Schumann resonances (~7.83 Hz)

VLF (Very Low Frequency) — 3 – 30 kHz

FrequencyWavelengthApplicationsCharacteristics
3 – 30 kHz100 km – 10 kmNavigation, time signals, submarine commsGlobal propagation, penetrates seawater

Your Equipment: HackRF Pro + Ham It Up upconverter

Typical Modulation: CW (Morse code), MSK, FSK — Very narrow bandwidth (<100 Hz)

Ideal Antenna: Large loop antenna (10–100 m circumference) or long wire (500 m+)

Why: VLF wavelengths are enormous (10–100 km), requiring electrically-small antennas. Loops provide good magnetic field coupling while long wires maximise electric field capture. Ground-mounted radials essential for efficiency.

Key Frequencies:

  • 60 kHz — MSF (UK), WWVB (USA) time signals
  • 77.5 kHz — DCF77 (Germany) time signal
  • 19–23 kHz — Military submarine communications
CHAPTER 02

Long Wave Bands (30 kHz – 3 MHz)

LF (Low Frequency) — 30 – 300 kHz

FrequencyWavelengthApplicationsCharacteristics
30 – 300 kHz10 km – 1 kmNavigation beacons, AM broadcasting (some regions)Ground wave propagation, long-range

Your Equipment: HackRF Pro + Ham It Up, or RSPdx (if available)

Ideal Antenna: Large loop (5–50 m diameter), long wire (100–500 m), or Beverage antenna

Why: Loop antennas offer directional nulling for noise reduction. Beverage (traveling-wave) antennas provide excellent directivity for DXing. Long wires work well with tuners but need substantial real estate.

Key Frequencies:

  • 135.7–137.8 kHz — Amateur 2200 m band
  • 148.5–283.5 kHz — Longwave AM broadcasting (Europe)
  • 190–435 kHz — Aeronautical/marine navigation beacons

MF (Medium Frequency) — 300 kHz – 3 MHz

FrequencyWavelengthApplicationsCharacteristics
300 kHz – 3 MHz1 km – 100 mAM broadcasting, maritime mobile, amateur 160 mGround wave + skywave, day/night propagation changes

Your Equipment: HackRF Pro + Ham It Up, EFHW antenna

Ideal Antenna: Vertical monopole with radials (λ/4), loop, or random wire with tuner

Why: Verticals provide low-angle radiation for DX. Quarter-wave verticals (40–120 m tall) with radial systems are highly effective. Random wires (20–40 m) work well with antenna tuners for multi-band operation.

Key Frequencies:

  • 530–1710 kHz — AM broadcast band (MW)
  • 1.8–2.0 MHz — Amateur 160 m band
  • 2–3 MHz — Maritime mobile, tropical broadcasting

Ideal Antennas:

  • Dipole: Half-wave dipole (λ/2) — Simple, effective, 50 Ω feedpoint. Height matters: higher = better DX
  • EFHW (End-Fed Half-Wave): Convenient single-support mounting, 49:1 transformer for matching
  • Vertical: Quarter-wave (λ/4) with radials — Omnidirectional, good for DX, compact footprint
  • Beam (Yagi): 3+ elements for directional gain (6–12 dBi). Requires rotator but excellent for DX
  • Inverted-V: Dipole variant, single high support point, quieter pattern than horizontal

Why HF is Special: Ionospheric reflection enables worldwide communications. Antenna height and orientation determine whether you work local (NVIS) or DX. Horizontal antennas favour high-angle (local), vertical antennas favour low-angle (DX).

Ideal Antennas by Use:

  • 2 m / 70 cm FM: Vertical λ/4 ground plane or 5/8λ — Omnidirectional for repeaters and mobile
  • Airband: Dipole or discone — Omnidirectional for aircraft reception
  • Weather Satellites: QFH (Quadrifilar Helix) or turnstile — RHCP for optimal satellite reception, wide beamwidth
  • SSB / Weak Signal: Yagi beam (5–15 elements) — High gain (10–15 dBi) for tropo, satellites, EME
  • Broadband Monitoring: Discone — 2:1 frequency range, vertical polarisation, 360° coverage

Why Line-of-Sight Matters: VHF doesn't refract in the ionosphere. Range = optical horizon (~30–100 miles depending on height). Antenna height is critical: every metre higher extends range. Sporadic-E and meteor scatter can enable occasional long-distance contacts.

Ideal Antennas by Application:

  • 70 cm FM/Digital: Collinear (λ/4 phased) or 5/8λ vertical — Higher gain than simple λ/4, still omnidirectional
  • ADS-B (1090 MHz): Collinear or 1/4λ ground plane — Vertical polarisation, omnidirectional for 360° aircraft coverage
  • GPS (1575 MHz): Patch antenna (RHCP) — Receives from multiple satellites, ceramic patch for compactness
  • WiFi 2.4 GHz: Patch, dipole, or Yagi — Depends on use (omni vs. directional link)
  • Satellite L-Band: Helical (RHCP) 7–15 turns — Circular polarisation critical for rotating satellites
  • Broadband UHF: Log-periodic (LPDA) — Directional, 2:1+ bandwidth, good for TV/scanning

UHF Characteristics: Shorter wavelengths enable smaller, more portable antennas. Requires direct line-of-sight. Easily blocked by buildings but better building penetration than VHF. Ideal for data links due to wider available bandwidth. Circular polarisation common for satellite work to handle Faraday rotation.

Ideal Antennas for Microwave:

  • Parabolic Dish: 0.3–3 m diameter — High gain (20–40+ dBi), narrow beamwidth for satellite TV, point-to-point links
  • Horn Antenna: Pyramidal or conical — Standard gain reference, excellent for feeds, 10–25 dBi typical
  • Patch Array: Multiple patches on PCB — Phased for beamforming, WiFi 5 GHz, automotive radar
  • Helical (Axial Mode): 10–20 turns — RHCP/LHCP for satellites, 12–20 dBi, wide bandwidth
  • Cassegrain Dish: Dual-reflector system — Very high gain, lower feed line loss than prime-focus

Microwave Challenges: Atmospheric absorption increases with frequency (especially K-band water absorption at 22 GHz). Rain fade is significant. Extremely narrow beamwidths require precise pointing. Waveguide feeds common above 10 GHz. Surface accuracy critical — dish must be smooth to λ/16 or better.
