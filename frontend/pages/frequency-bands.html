Frequency Wavelength Applications Characteristics

Notable: 50/60 Hz power lines (50 Hz in UK), Schumann resonances (~7.83 Hz)

VLF (Very Low Frequency) - 3 - 30 kHz

Frequency Wavelength Applications Characteristics
3 - 30 kHz 100 km - 10 km Navigation, time signals, submarine comms Global propagation, penetrates seawater

Your Equipment: HackRF Pro + Ham It Up upconverter

Typical Modulation: CW (Morse code), MSK, FSK - Very narrow bandwidth (<100 Hz)

Ideal Antenna: Large loop antenna (10-100m circumference) or long wire (500m+)

Why: VLF wavelengths are enormous (10-100km), requiring electrically-small antennas. Loops provide good magnetic field coupling while long wires maximize electric field capture. Ground-mounted radials essential for efficiency.

Key Frequencies:

  • 60 kHz - MSF (UK), WWVB (USA) time signals
  • 77.5 kHz - DCF77 (Germany) time signal
  • 19-23 kHz - Military submarine communications

üì° Long Wave Bands (30 kHz - 3 MHz)

LF (Low Frequency) - 30 - 300 kHz

Frequency Wavelength Applications Characteristics
30 - 300 kHz 10 km - 1 km Navigation beacons, AM broadcasting (some regions) Ground wave propagation, long-range

Your Equipment: HackRF Pro + Ham It Up, or RSPdx (if available)

Ideal Antenna: Large loop (5-50m diameter), long wire (100-500m), or Beverage antenna

Why: Loop antennas offer directional nulling for noise reduction. Beverage (traveling-wave) antennas provide excellent directivity for DXing. Long wires work well with tuners but need substantial real estate.

Key Frequencies:

  • 135.7-137.8 kHz - Amateur 2200m band
  • 148.5-283.5 kHz - Longwave AM broadcasting (Europe)
  • 190-435 kHz - Aeronautical/marine navigation beacons

MF (Medium Frequency) - 300 kHz - 3 MHz

Frequency Wavelength Applications Characteristics
300 kHz - 3 MHz 1 km - 100 m AM broadcasting, maritime mobile, amateur 160m Ground wave + skywave, day/night propagation changes

Your Equipment: HackRF Pro + Ham It Up, EFHW antenna

Ideal Antenna: Vertical monopole with radials (Œª/4), loop, or random wire with tuner

Why: Verticals provide low-angle radiation for DX. Quarter-wave verticals (40-120m tall) with radial systems are highly effective. Random wires (20-40m) work well with antenna tuners for multi-band operation.

Key Frequencies:

  • 530-1710 kHz - AM broadcast band (MW)
  • 1.8-2.0 MHz - Amateur 160m band
  • 2-3 MHz - Maritime mobile, tropical broadcasting

Ideal Antennas:

Why HF is Special: Ionospheric reflection enables worldwide communications. Antenna height and orientation determine whether you work local (NVIS) or DX. Horizontal antennas favor high-angle (local), vertical antennas favor low-angle (DX).

Ideal Antennas by Use:

  • 2m/70cm FM: Vertical Œª/4 ground plane or 5/8Œª - Omnidirectional for repeaters and mobile
  • Airband: Dipole or discone - Omnidirectional for aircraft reception

Why Line-of-Sight Matters: VHF doesn't refract in ionosphere. Range = optical horizon (~30-100 miles depending on height). Antenna height is critical: every meter higher extends range. Sporadic-E and meteor scatter can enable occasional long-distance contacts.

Ideal Antennas by Application:

  • 70cm FM/Digital: Collinear (Œª/4 phased) or 5/8Œª vertical - Higher gain than simple Œª/4, still omnidirectional
  • ADS-B (1090 MHz): Collinear or 1/4Œª ground plane - Vertical polarization, omnidirectional for 360¬∞ aircraft coverage
  • GPS (1575 MHz): Patch antenna (RHCP) - Receives from multiple satellites, ceramic patch for compactness
  • WiFi 2.4 GHz: Patch, dipole, or Yagi - Depends on use (omni vs. directional link)
  • Satellite L-Band: Helical (RHCP) 7-15 turns - Circular polarization critical for rotating satellites
  • Broadband UHF: Log-periodic (LPDA) - Directional, 2:1+ bandwidth, good for TV/scanning

UHF Characteristics: Shorter wavelengths enable smaller, more portable antennas. Requires direct line-of-sight. Easily blocked by buildings but better building penetration than VHF. Ideal for data links due to wider available bandwidth. Circular polarization common for satellite work to handle Faraday rotation.

Ideal Antennas for Microwave:

  • Parabolic Dish: 0.3-3m diameter - High gain (20-40+ dBi), narrow beamwidth for satellite TV, point-to-point links
  • Horn Antenna: Pyramidal or conical - Standard gain reference, excellent for feeds, 10-25 dBi typical
  • Patch Array: Multiple patches on PCB - Phased for beamforming, WiFi 5GHz, automotive radar
  • Helical (Axial Mode): 10-20 turns - RHCP/LHCP for satellites, 12-20 dBi, wide bandwidth
  • Cassegrain Dish: Dual-reflector system - Very high gain, lower feed line loss than prime-focus

Microwave Challenges: Atmospheric absorption increases with frequency (especially K-band water absorption at 22 GHz). Rain fade is significant. Extremely narrow beamwidths require precise pointing. Waveguide feeds common above 10 GHz. Surface accuracy critical - dish must be smooth to Œª/16 or better.
