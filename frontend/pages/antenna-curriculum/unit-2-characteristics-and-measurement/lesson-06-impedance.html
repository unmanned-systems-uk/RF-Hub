Impedance: The Antenna's Personality

Understanding R + jX, radiation resistance, reactance, and why 50 Ω is the universal standard

Unit 2 — Characteristics & Measurement Prerequisites: Unit 1 complete Equipment: None (theory — VNA work in Lesson 10) ⏱ 30 min Intermediate
CHAPTER 01

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson you will be able to:

  • Define antenna impedance as resistance + reactance (R + jX)
  • Explain the difference between radiation resistance and loss resistance
  • Describe why impedance changes with frequency
  • Explain why 50 Ω is the standard reference impedance
CHAPTER 02

What is Impedance?

💡
The Big Idea: Impedance is the complete description of how an antenna interacts with electrical energy. It tells you not just whether the antenna resists current, but whether it stores and returns energy too. Getting impedance right is the key to efficient power transfer.

At DC, we describe circuits using resistance (R). At RF, we need a richer description — impedance (Z) — which captures both how energy is consumed and how it's temporarily stored:

Z = R + jX

where:
  R = resistance (Ω) — energy dissipated or radiated
  X = reactance (Ω) — energy stored and returned
  j = √(-1) — indicates the reactive (imaginary) component

R — Resistance

Energy consumed per cycle. In an antenna, this splits into radiation resistance (energy radiated as EM waves — useful!) and loss resistance (energy wasted as heat — bad).

jX — Reactance

Energy stored and returned each cycle. Positive X = inductive (magnetic energy storage). Negative X = capacitive (electric energy storage). At resonance, X = 0.

The Water Pipe Analogy

Think of impedance like a water pipe system:

  • Resistance = friction in the pipe (energy permanently lost as heat)
  • Reactance = springs attached to the pipe (energy stored and released, not lost)
  • Impedance = the combined effect of both on water flow

An antenna "feels" like a specific combination of resistance and reactance to its feedline. Matching these to the feedline's impedance is the job of an antenna tuner — or good antenna design.

CHAPTER 03

Radiation Resistance

Radiation resistance (Rrad) is the portion of an antenna's resistance that represents power actually radiated as electromagnetic waves. It's not a physical resistor — it's a mathematical way of expressing the fact that the antenna is "consuming" power by converting it to radio waves.

⚡
Half-wave dipole in free space: Rrad ≈ 73 Ω at resonance. This is the reference value quoted in antenna textbooks and the basis for the 50 Ω standard.

Radiation resistance determines antenna efficiency. An antenna is efficient when radiation resistance is high relative to loss resistance:

Efficiency (η) = R_rad / (R_rad + R_loss)

Example: R_rad = 73 Ω, R_loss = 1 Ω → η = 73/74 = 98.6% — excellent
Example: R_rad = 1 Ω,  R_loss = 5 Ω → η = 1/6 = 16.7% — poor

Small antennas (much shorter than λ/2) have very low radiation resistance — sometimes less than 1 Ω. Even tiny resistive losses then dominate, making them inefficient. This is a fundamental physics constraint, not an engineering deficiency.

CHAPTER 04

Loss Resistance

Loss resistance (Rloss) represents power that enters the antenna system but exits as heat rather than radio waves. It comes from several sources:

Loss Source Where How to Minimise
Conductor ohmic loss The antenna wire/element itself Use thicker conductor; copper > aluminium > steel
Ground/counterpoise loss Verticals, monopoles Install more/longer ground radials
Proximity losses Nearby conductors, structures Keep antenna clear of metal objects
Dielectric losses Insulators, traps, coils Use quality components; avoid wet insulation
Connector/feedpoint losses Coax connector, balun Use weatherproof connectors; good balun design
⚠️
Ground losses are significant for verticals. A quarter-wave vertical with no ground radials may have Rloss of 10–30 Ω, dramatically cutting efficiency. Installing 32+ radials of λ/4 length can reduce this to near zero. This is why serious HF operators spend time on their ground systems.
CHAPTER 05

Reactance and Resonance

Reactance is the frequency-dependent part of impedance. Unlike resistance, it doesn't consume energy permanently — it stores it (as electric or magnetic field) and releases it again each cycle.

For an antenna, reactance varies strongly with frequency:

Frequency vs. Resonance Reactance (X) Antenna Behaviour
Below resonant frequency Negative (−jX) Capacitive — antenna acts like a capacitor (too short electrically)
At resonant frequency Zero (X = 0) Purely resistive — maximum energy transfer possible
Above resonant frequency Positive (+jX) Inductive — antenna acts like an inductor (too long electrically)
🔑
Resonance is the frequency where X = 0. The antenna's impedance is purely resistive. This is the natural operating frequency — maximum power transfer with no reactive component to match out. A well-designed antenna operates at or near resonance on its intended band.

You can shift resonance by changing antenna length:

  • Shorten the antenna → resonance moves up in frequency, but now capacitive below that new resonance
  • Lengthen the antenna → resonance moves down in frequency
  • Add a loading coil → adds inductance, which shifts resonance down (used in mobile antennas to shorten physical length)

CHAPTER 06

Why 50 Ω?

Almost every RF system — transmitters, receivers, VNAs, coaxial cables — is designed around 50 Ω. This isn't arbitrary. It's a historical compromise between two competing physical requirements:

Maximum Power Handling

Coaxial cable transmits the most power before voltage breakdown at approximately 30 Ω impedance.

Minimum Signal Loss

Coaxial cable has minimum ohmic attenuation at approximately 77 Ω impedance.

The Compromise

50 Ω — the geometric mean — balances power handling and low loss. Chosen for military/transmitting use. The standard has been universal ever since.

📺
What about 75 Ω? Cable TV and broadcast receive systems use 75 Ω coax (RG-6, RG-59). At 75 Ω, attenuation is minimised — ideal for receive-only systems where signal loss matters more than power handling. That's why your TV aerial uses 75 Ω coax while your transmit radio uses 50 Ω.

The practical consequence: when your antenna's feedpoint impedance is 50 Ω, energy flows from transmitter to antenna with no reflection. When it's not 50 Ω, some power reflects back — which is exactly what SWR measures. (More in Lesson 7.)

Lesson 6 Summary
  • Impedance Z = R + jX — resistance consumes energy, reactance stores and returns it
  • Radiation resistance represents power radiated as EM waves — the "useful" resistance
  • Loss resistance represents power wasted as heat — minimise with good conductors and ground systems
  • Below resonance: capacitive (−jX). At resonance: purely resistive (X = 0). Above resonance: inductive (+jX)
  • 50 Ω is a compromise between maximum power handling (~30 Ω) and minimum loss (~77 Ω)
  • Efficiency = Rrad / (Rrad + Rloss) — always maximise radiation resistance relative to loss
