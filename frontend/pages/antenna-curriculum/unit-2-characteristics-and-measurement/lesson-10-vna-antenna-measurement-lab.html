VNA Antenna Measurement Lab

Put theory into practice — calibrate the RSA5065N, measure real antennas, and interpret the results

Unit 2 — Characteristics & Measurement Prerequisites: Lessons 6–9 + VNA calibration knowledge Equipment: RSA5065N, calibration kit, antennas ⏱ 60–90 min (hands-on) Intermediate
CHAPTER 01

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lab session you will be able to:

  • Perform a 1-port SOL calibration on the RSA5065N
  • Measure S11 (return loss) of a real antenna
  • Read SWR, impedance, and Smith chart data from the VNA display
  • Determine an antenna's resonant frequency, bandwidth, and feedpoint impedance

CHAPTER 02

Pre-Lab Review (10 min)

Before touching the VNA, confirm you can answer these questions from the previous lessons:

From Lesson 7

S11 = Γ. The VNA measures the reflection coefficient. Return loss is −20×log₁₀(|Γ|). SWR is derived from |Γ|.

From Lesson 8

Smith chart: centre = 50 Ω match. Upper half = inductive. Lower half = capacitive. Crossing real axis = resonance.

Why Calibration?

The VNA measures S11 at its port connector. Everything between the connector and the antenna (cable, adapters) must be mathematically removed by SOL calibration.

What S11 Can and Cannot Tell You

  • S11 CAN tell you: Resonant frequency, feedpoint impedance (R + jX), SWR, bandwidth, return loss
  • S11 CANNOT tell you: Antenna gain, radiation efficiency, radiation pattern, far-field performance

S11 is a single-port measurement — it captures everything about the electrical interface between feedline and antenna. What happens in the far field requires a different measurement (a 2-antenna gain test or a full antenna range).

CHAPTER 03

Lab Step 1: VNA Calibration

📐
SOL calibration (Short-Open-Load) establishes the measurement reference plane by characterising three known standards. The VNA uses the difference between expected and measured values to correct for cable and connector imperfections.
STEP 1.1

Set frequency range

  1. Power on RSA5065N. Press MODE → select VNA
  2. Press FREQ → set Start frequency and Stop frequency for your target band
  3. Suggested ranges: 100–200 MHz for 2m band, 25–1300 MHz for wideband, 400–500 MHz for 70cm
  4. Set number of points to 201 (or higher for more resolution)
Note: Set the calibration range slightly wider than your measurement range to ensure accurate results at the band edges.
STEP 1.2

Connect the SHORT standard

  1. Attach the SHORT calibration standard to Port 1 (or to the end of your measurement cable)
  2. Press CAL → Start Cal → Port 1 → Short
  3. Wait for the VNA to sweep and store the Short measurement
Caution: Handle calibration standards by the body, not the connector. Finger oils and mechanical damage degrade calibration accuracy.
STEP 1.3

Connect the OPEN standard

  1. Remove the Short. Attach the OPEN calibration standard
  2. Press Open in the calibration menu
  3. Wait for the sweep to complete
STEP 1.4

Connect the LOAD standard (50 Ω)

  1. Remove the Open. Attach the 50 Ω LOAD standard
  2. Press Load in the calibration menu
  3. Wait for the sweep to complete. Press Done or Apply
STEP 1.5

Verify calibration quality

  1. Leave the LOAD standard connected
  2. Check S11 display — it should show < −40 dB (ideally −50 dB or better) across the entire band
  3. Switch to Smith chart view — the trace should be a dot near the centre
  4. If S11 > −35 dB, repeat calibration — check for dirty or damaged connectors

CHAPTER 04

Lab Step 2: Measure the Diamond D130N Discone

The D130N discone is designed to cover 25–1300 MHz — an extraordinary 52:1 frequency ratio. At no single frequency will it be a perfect 50 Ω match, but it should maintain reasonable SWR across its operating range.

STEP 2.1

Set up for wideband sweep

  1. Set frequency range: 25 MHz – 1300 MHz
  2. Re-calibrate if your previous calibration used a different range
  3. Connect the D130N feedline to Port 1 via your measurement cable
  4. Ensure the antenna is positioned away from metallic objects and is at normal operating height if possible
STEP 2.2

Record S11 and SWR plots

  1. Switch display to S11 (Return Loss) view — take a screenshot
  2. Switch to SWR display — take a screenshot
  3. Add a marker at the SWR minimum — note frequency and SWR value
  4. Use the "SWR < 2:1" threshold line to identify usable bandwidth regions
  5. Note any resonant dips (SWR minima) and record their frequencies

STEP 2.3

Record Smith chart trace

  1. Switch to Smith chart display
  2. Observe the full 25–1300 MHz trace. It should spiral around the chart as frequency increases
  3. Add an SWR 2:1 reference circle overlay
  4. Note which frequency regions keep the trace inside the 2:1 circle
  5. Screenshot the Smith chart
CHAPTER 05

Lab Step 3: Measure a Simple Antenna

Choose one additional antenna: a dipole, monopole whip, rubber duck, or similar narrowband antenna. A simple resonant antenna produces a cleaner, more educational S11 trace.

STEP 3.1

Predict before measuring

  1. Identify the antenna's target frequency (check marking or measure physical length)
  2. If a dipole, calculate expected resonance: f = 300 / (2 × length_in_metres) MHz
  3. Write down your prediction before you measure
☐ Antenna type: _____ ☐ Physical length: _____ cm ☐ Predicted resonant frequency: _____ MHz
STEP 3.2

Sweep around expected resonance

  1. Set a narrower frequency range: predicted resonance ±30%
  2. Re-calibrate for this range (always calibrate for your actual sweep range)
  3. Connect the antenna and sweep
  4. Find the resonant frequency: SWR minimum on the SWR display, or where S11 is most negative
  5. On the Smith chart, find where the trace crosses the real axis — this is resonance
STEP 3.3

Record key measurements

  1. Place a marker at resonance. Record: frequency, S11 (dB), SWR, impedance (R + jX)
  2. Determine bandwidth: frequencies where SWR rises to 2:1 (note lower and upper limits)
  3. Calculate: Bandwidth = upper − lower, % BW = (BW / f_resonant) × 100
  4. Read the impedance from the Smith chart or numeric display

CHAPTER 06

Lab Step 4: Document and Compare

STEP 4.1

Compile your measurement record

  1. Collect all screenshots from the VNA (S11 plot, SWR plot, Smith chart trace)
  2. Annotate each: add labels for resonant frequency, SWR minimum, bandwidth limits
  3. Create a comparison table: predicted vs. measured values
  4. Note any discrepancies and consider why they occurred
STEP 4.2

Discuss discrepancies

Common reasons your measured resonant frequency differs from predicted:

  • Mounting effects: proximity to metal mast, roof, or ground changes effective electrical length
  • Velocity factor: insulation around wire elements slows wave propagation, shifting resonance down
  • End effects: a real dipole element has capacitance at its tips, reducing effective resonant length by ~5%
  • Measurement cable: even after calibration, minor errors remain if cable is flexed between cal and measurement
  • Temperature: metal conductors expand/contract, changing length slightly
STEP 4.3

Compare to specifications

  1. For the D130N: look up the manufacturer SWR specification (Diamond publish this)
  2. For your simple antenna: if it's a commercial product, check the datasheet
  3. Build a comparison table: spec vs. measured for each key parameter
  4. Write one or two sentences assessing whether each antenna performs to specification

🏆 Unit 2 Capstone: Full Antenna Characterisation Report

You've completed the formal lab steps. Now produce a complete characterisation report for one antenna of your choice.

Deliverables
