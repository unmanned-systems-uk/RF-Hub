Gain, Directivity, and Efficiency

The relationship between pattern focus and power conversion — plus how to spot exaggerated marketing claims

Unit 2 — Characteristics & Measurement Prerequisites: Lessons 3, 6 Equipment: None ⏱ 25 min Intermediate
CHAPTER 01

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson you will be able to:

  • Define directivity, gain, and realised gain
  • Use the relationship Gain = Efficiency × Directivity
  • Convert between dBi and dBd (dBi = dBd + 2.15)
  • Spot unrealistic gain claims in product marketing
CHAPTER 02

Directivity: Pattern Focus

💡
The Big Idea: Gain, directivity, and efficiency are three related but distinct properties. Understanding their relationship is essential for comparing antennas fairly and calculating link budgets accurately.

Directivity describes how focused an antenna's radiation pattern is compared to an isotropic radiator. It's a pure pattern property — it doesn't say anything about losses. An antenna with high directivity concentrates its radiated energy into a narrow beam.

Directivity (D) = Maximum radiation intensity / Average radiation intensity

In dBi: D(dBi) = 10 × log₁₀(D_linear)

Reference values:
  Isotropic radiator:    D = 0 dBi  (by definition)
  Half-wave dipole:      D = 2.15 dBi
  3-element Yagi:        D ≈ 7–8 dBi
  10-element Yagi:       D ≈ 12 dBi
  Parabolic dish (1m):   D ≈ 22 dBi at 10 GHz

Directivity is determined entirely by the radiation pattern shape. A perfect antenna (100% efficient, no losses) has gain equal to its directivity. A real antenna always has gain lower than directivity due to losses.

CHAPTER 03

Efficiency: Power In vs Power Radiated

Antenna efficiency (η) is the fraction of input power that actually gets radiated as EM waves:

η = P_radiated / P_input

In terms of resistance (from Lesson 6):
  η = R_rad / (R_rad + R_loss)

Expressed as a percentage or linear ratio (0 to 1)
  η = 1.0  →  100% efficient (no losses)
  η = 0.5  →  50% efficient (half the power lost as heat)

Efficiency is degraded by:

  • Conductor ohmic losses (thin wire, resistive material)
  • Ground losses (verticals without proper radial system)
  • Dielectric losses (wet insulators, loading coil cores)
  • Proximity coupling to lossy structures
⚠️
Small antennas struggle with efficiency. A "shortened" HF antenna (e.g., a 1.5 m whip for 40m band) may have radiation resistance of only 1–2 Ω. Even a modest 2 Ω of loss resistance then gives efficiency of only 33–50%. This is the fundamental challenge of electrically small antennas — not an engineering failure, but a physics constraint.
CHAPTER 04

Gain: The Practical Number

Gain combines directivity and efficiency into the single number that matters for system calculations:

Gain
=
Directivity
×
Efficiency (η)
In dB: G(dBi) = D(dBi) + 10×log₁₀(η)

Example:
  Dipole directivity = 2.15 dBi
  Efficiency = 95% (η = 0.95)
  Gain = 2.15 + 10×log₁₀(0.95) = 2.15 − 0.22 = 1.93 dBi

Gain is what you use in link budget calculations — it tells you how strong a signal will be at the receiver for a given transmit power. Directivity without efficiency information is incomplete for system design.

Realised Gain

Realised gain goes one step further — it also accounts for impedance mismatch loss:

G_realised = G × (1 − |Γ|²)

A perfectly matched antenna (|Γ| = 0): G_realised = G
An antenna with SWR 2:1 (|Γ| = 0.33): G_realised = G × (1 − 0.11) = 0.89 × G ≈ −0.5 dB penalty

For most well-matched antennas, mismatch loss is small. It becomes significant when an antenna is operated far from resonance or with a poor match.

CHAPTER 05

dBi vs dBd: A Critical Distinction

Antenna gain can be quoted in two reference systems:

Unit Reference Relationship Common Use
dBi Isotropic radiator (theoretical) The absolute standard Engineering, VNA software, data sheets
dBd Half-wave dipole (2.15 dBi) dBi = dBd + 2.15 Amateur radio, some commercial antenna specs 
Conversion: G(dBi) = G(dBd) + 2.15

Example: A Yagi quoted at 9 dBd
  G(dBi) = 9 + 2.15 = 11.15 dBi

Example: A collinear quoted at 5 dBi
  G(dBd) = 5 − 2.15 = 2.85 dBd
⚠️
Marketing alert: Some manufacturers quote gain in dBd to make the number smaller (and seem more conservative) or in dBi to make it larger. Always check which unit is being used before comparing antennas. An antenna claiming "5 dB gain" with no unit specified is ambiguous.
CHAPTER 06

Effective Aperture

On receive, an antenna doesn't collect signal from just its physical area — it collects from an effective aperture (Aeff) that depends on its gain:

A_eff = (λ² × G) / (4π)

where:
  λ = wavelength (metres)
  G = gain (linear, not dB)
  A_eff = effective aperture (square metres)

This is why gain matters for receive performance: a higher-gain antenna "captures" signal from a larger effective area, even though its physical size may be unchanged.

Antenna Gain (dBi) Aeff at 145 MHz (λ = 2.07m)
Isotropic 0 dBi 0.34 m²
Half-wave dipole 2.15 dBi 0.57 m²
3-el Yagi 7.5 dBi 1.90 m²
9-el Yagi 12.5 dBi 6.02 m²

A 9-element Yagi at 145 MHz "sees" about 10× more signal-collecting area than a dipole, even though the Yagi isn't 10× physically larger. The beam focus is what creates the apparent larger aperture.

CHAPTER 07

Beware Unrealistic Gain Claims

Physics places hard limits on antenna gain based on size and type. When a product claims extraordinary gain from a small, simple antenna, scepticism is warranted.

Realistic Gain Reference

Antenna Type Realistic Gain Range Notes
Rubber duck (handheld) −1 to 2 dBi Short, lossy — convenience over performance
Half-wave dipole 2.15 dBi Theoretical reference — hard to exceed with simple wire
Discone (D130N) 0 to 2 dBi Broadband trade-off limits gain
Collinear (2m/70cm) 3 to 7 dBi Depends on number of elements stacked
3-el Yagi 6 to 8 dBi Well-understood, easily verified
10-el Yagi 12 to 14 dBi Long boom required
1m dish at 10 GHz ~22 dBi High gain requires short wavelength
🚩
Red flags to watch for:
  • A small rubber duck or stubby antenna claiming >5 dBi
  • A "5G booster" claiming 20+ dBi with no boom or dish
  • Gain quoted without specifying dBi or dBd
  • Manufacturer gain figures that exceed physics limits for the antenna's physical size
Rule of thumb: if the claimed gain seems too good for the antenna's size, it probably is.

Lesson 9 Summary
  • Directivity = pattern focus only (no losses). Half-wave dipole = 2.15 dBi
  • Efficiency = Pradiated / Pinput = Rrad / (Rrad + Rloss)
  • Gain = Directivity × Efficiency — the number used in link budgets
  • Realised gain also includes mismatch loss: G × (1 − |Γ|²)
  • dBi = dBd + 2.15 always — check which unit a manufacturer uses
  • Effective aperture: Aeff = λ²G / 4π — higher gain = larger capture area
  • Physics limits gain for a given antenna size — scepticism about marketing claims is warranted
