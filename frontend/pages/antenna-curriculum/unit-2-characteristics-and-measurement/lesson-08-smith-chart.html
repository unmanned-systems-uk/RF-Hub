The Smith Chart: Your RF Roadmap

Visual impedance geometry, SWR circles, frequency sweeps — the tool invented in 1939 still on every VNA

Unit 2 — Characteristics & Measurement Prerequisites: Lessons 6–7 Equipment: None (pairs with VNA work in Lesson 10) ⏱ 35 min Intermediate
CHAPTER 01

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson you will be able to:

  • Read impedance (R + jX) from a Smith chart
  • Identify key points: centre (50 Ω match), open circuit, short circuit
  • Determine if impedance is inductive or capacitive from chart position
  • Trace a frequency sweep on a Smith chart and interpret the shape
CHAPTER 02

Why the Smith Chart Exists

💡
The Big Idea: Impedance calculations at RF involve complex numbers — awkward to plot on regular graph paper. Phillip Smith realised in 1939 that a particular conformal mapping could convert these complex impedance values into a circle, where all mathematically valid impedances appear as points within a single unit disk. The result was visual, intuitive, and could be used without a calculator.

Despite being invented before computers, the Smith chart remains the standard display for RF impedance data. Every VNA — including the Rigol RSA5065N — has a Smith chart display mode. Once you understand it, you can read impedance, SWR, and matching information at a glance.

Without Smith Chart

Complex maths: calculate Γ from Z, then compute SWR and return loss, check each frequency point separately.

With Smith Chart

Look at the screen: position reveals impedance, distance from centre shows match quality, shape of trace shows frequency behaviour.

CHAPTER 03

The Chart Layout

A Smith chart is a circle containing a complex grid of overlapping circles and arcs. The key structural elements:

Constant Resistance Circles

Circles of varying size, all tangent to the right edge of the chart. Moving along a circle keeps resistance (R) constant while reactance (X) changes. The largest circle spans the entire chart (R = 0, purely reactive). The smallest circles are near the right edge (high R).

Constant Reactance Arcs

Arcs that cross the resistance circles. The horizontal axis through the centre is the purely resistive line (X = 0). Arcs above the centre line represent positive (inductive) reactance; arcs below represent negative (capacitive) reactance.

Key Landmark Points

CENTRE

50 Ω (normalised: 1 + j0) — the perfect match point. An antenna with Z = 50 + j0 Ω plots exactly here. SWR = 1:1.

RIGHT EDGE

Open circuit (Z = ∞) — infinite impedance. Nothing connected to the port, or a series resonant element at anti-resonance.

LEFT EDGE

Short circuit (Z = 0) — zero impedance. Port directly shorted. Calibration reference.

UPPER HALF

Inductive reactance (+jX) — impedance has positive imaginary component. Antenna is electrically too long or has a loading coil.

LOWER HALF

Capacitive reactance (−jX) — impedance has negative imaginary component. Antenna is electrically too short.

HORIZONTAL AXIS

Pure resistance (X = 0) — the real axis. Resonance occurs where a frequency sweep crosses this line.

CHAPTER 04

Reading Impedance from the Chart

Every point inside the Smith chart circle represents a unique impedance value. Modern VNAs display the impedance numerically alongside the chart, but understanding the visual representation builds intuition.

Normalised Impedance

The Smith chart uses normalised impedance — the actual impedance divided by the reference impedance (Z₀, usually 50 Ω). This makes the chart universal, valid for any reference impedance.

Normalised impedance: z = Z / Z₀

Example: Z = 100 + j50 Ω, Z₀ = 50 Ω
  z = (100 + j50) / 50 = 2 + j1

To read actual impedance: multiply normalised value by Z₀
  z = 2 + j1 → Z = (2 × 50) + j(1 × 50) = 100 + j50 Ω

Quick Position Guide

Chart Position What It Means Example (Z₀ = 50 Ω)
Exact centre Perfect 50 Ω match Z = 50 + j0
Centre, upper half Resistive + inductive Z = 50 + j30 (inductive)
Centre, lower half Resistive + capacitive Z = 50 − j30 (capacitive)
Right of centre, on horizontal axis Pure resistance > 50 Ω Z = 100 + j0
Left of centre, on horizontal axis Pure resistance < 50 Ω Z = 25 + j0
Near right edge Very high impedance Z = 1000 + j0 (open-ish)
CHAPTER 05

SWR Circles on the Smith Chart

A key property of the Smith chart: constant SWR traces a circle centred on the chart centre. This is one of the most useful facts in RF engineering.

  • A point at the exact centre is on the SWR 1:1 circle (a point, radius 0)
  • Points on a small circle around centre = low SWR (good match)
  • Points on a large circle extending toward the chart edge = high SWR (poor match)
  • The outer boundary of the chart (the rim) = infinite SWR (perfect reflection)
💡
Practical rule: If your antenna's impedance point falls inside the SWR 2:1 circle (roughly the inner third of the chart radius), your match is acceptable. The goal of impedance matching is to move the point toward the centre.

VNAs can overlay SWR circles on the Smith chart display. Set a circle at SWR 2:1 and you can instantly see whether any measurement falls within the acceptable zone.

CHAPTER 06

Frequency Sweeps on the Smith Chart

When a VNA sweeps across a range of frequencies, it plots each frequency's impedance point on the Smith chart. The result is a trace — a curve that tells the story of how the antenna's impedance evolves.

What the Trace Shape Tells You

Trace Shape What It Means
Small loop near chart centre Well-matched across a wide bandwidth — ideal
Large arc crossing the real axis Antenna resonates (crosses X=0 line) but mismatched in resistance — needs matching network
Clockwise rotation with frequency Normal behaviour — impedance rotates clockwise as frequency increases
Crossing the real axis from below Resonance point — capacitive (below) to inductive (above)
Tight cluster near right edge Very high impedance — open-circuit-like (e.g., open feedpoint, disconnected antenna)
Tight cluster at left edge Very low impedance — short-circuit-like (e.g., DC path to ground)
🔧
Tip: When measuring your antenna on the RSA5065N in Lesson 10, switch to Smith chart display and watch the trace as you change frequency. Find where the trace crosses the real axis — that's resonance. See how far the crossing point is from centre — that's how close the feedpoint resistance is to 50 Ω.

Lesson 8 Summary
  • The Smith chart maps complex impedance onto a circle — visual geometry replaces complex algebra
  • Centre = perfect 50 Ω match. Right edge = open circuit. Left edge = short circuit
  • Upper half = inductive (+jX). Lower half = capacitive (−jX). Horizontal axis = resonance
  • Constant SWR traces a circle centred on the chart — smaller circle = better match
  • A VNA frequency sweep creates a trace showing impedance evolution with frequency
  • Resonance is where the trace crosses the real (horizontal) axis
  • Use normalised impedance (z = Z/Z₀) to read the chart; multiply by Z₀ to get actual Ohms
