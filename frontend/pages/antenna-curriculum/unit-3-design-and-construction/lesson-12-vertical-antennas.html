CHAPTER 02

Monopole Theory: Image Antennas

💡
The Big Idea: A monopole is half a dipole. It uses a ground plane to create a "mirror image" of the missing half. The combination of the real vertical element above ground plus its reflection in the ground plane is electrically equivalent to a complete dipole. This is called image theory.

When a vertical antenna element is mounted over a perfectly conducting ground plane:

  • The ground plane reflects radio waves as if there were an identical element below it, pointing downward
  • The "image" antenna and the real antenna together form an equivalent dipole
  • The radiation pattern above the ground plane is identical to the upper half of a dipole pattern
  • The feedpoint impedance is exactly half that of a full dipole: ~36 Ω (vs 73 Ω for a dipole)
⚡
Key result: A quarter-wave vertical monopole over a perfect ground plane has feedpoint impedance of 36 Ω and gain of 5.15 dBi (3 dB more than a dipole in free space, because all radiation goes upward — none downward).

The practical implications:

  • 36 Ω is close enough to 50 Ω for many applications without matching
  • A perfect ground plane doesn't exist in practice — real grounds raise impedance toward 50 Ω
  • The ground plane quality determines efficiency — a poor ground means high loss resistance
CHAPTER 03

Quarter-Wave Vertical Design

Vertical element length (metres) = 71.25 / f(MHz)   [same as one dipole arm]
Radial length         (metres) = 71.25 / f(MHz)   [same as vertical element]

For 145 MHz:
  Vertical element = 71,250 / 145 ≈ 491 mm ≈ 49.1 cm
  Each radial      = 71,250 / 145 ≈ 491 mm ≈ 49.1 cm

For 440 MHz (70cm band):
  Vertical element = 71,250 / 440 ≈ 162 mm ≈ 16.2 cm
  Each radial      = 71,250 / 440 ≈ 162 mm ≈ 16.2 cm

The vertical element connects to the centre pin of the SO-239 (or N-type) connector. The radials connect to the connector body (ground/shield).

🔑
The vertical element is unbalanced — the element is the "hot" side, the ground plane provides the return path. Unlike a dipole, no balun is needed for a correctly designed ground-plane vertical fed with coax. The coax shield IS the ground connection.
CHAPTER 04

Ground Radials: How Many, How Long

Radials provide the return current path for the vertical element. They substitute for an actual ground connection — critical for elevated installations and for efficiency on all installations.

Number of RadialsEffective Ground Loss (Rloss)Application
4 (minimum)~15 ΩPortable/temporary — acceptable compromise
8~8 ΩLightweight base antenna — good for VHF/UHF
16~4 ΩPermanent installation — recommended minimum
32~2 ΩHigh-performance HF vertical base station
120 (buried)~0.5 ΩProfessional broadcast station — approaching perfect ground

For VHF/UHF ground-plane antennas mounted above ground (portable or mast-mounted), 4 radials is a practical standard. The antenna is elevated anyway — the radials provide the image ground, not the earth below.

⚠️
HF ground-mounted verticals are different. For HF (3–30 MHz) verticals mounted at or near ground level, buried radials in the soil are the ground system. Radiation resistance can be as low as 2–10 Ω, so ground loss resistance dominates efficiency. Minimum 32 radials of λ/4 length are recommended for serious HF work.
CHAPTER 05

The Droop Angle Trick: Raising Impedance Toward 50 Ω

A quarter-wave vertical with horizontal radials has a feedpoint impedance of ~36 Ω — slightly below 50 Ω. By drooping the radials downward at an angle below horizontal, the impedance rises toward 50 Ω.

Radial Angle Below HorizontalFeedpoint Impedance (approx.)
0° (horizontal)36 Ω
30°42 Ω
45°50 Ω ← target!
60°60 Ω
💡
45° droop = 50 Ω. This is the standard construction for portable quarter-wave ground-plane antennas and explains the characteristic drooped radials you see on many commercial VHF/UHF ground-plane antennas. No matching network needed — just angle the radials downward at 45°.

The physical reason: drooping radials are no longer purely horizontal — they have a vertical component that effectively shortens the electrical image, shifting the impedance upward from 36 Ω.

CHAPTER 06

Elevated vs Ground-Mounted Verticals

Elevated (Mast-Mounted)

Antenna and radials above ground. Ground radials ARE the ground plane — no earth connection. Typical for VHF/UHF. 4–8 radials sufficient. No ground loss from soil. Pattern is clean omnidirectional. Easy to install, portable.

Ground-Mounted (Base at Earth)

Vertical element at or near ground level. Uses buried radials in earth as ground system. Typical for HF. Needs many radials (32+) to minimise loss. Low takeoff angle — good for DX. Permanent installation required for best results.

CHAPTER 07

Lab: Build a Quarter-Wave Ground Plane (145 or 440 MHz)

STEP 1

Choose frequency and calculate dimensions

  1. Choose: 145 MHz (2m) or 440 MHz (70cm)
  2. Calculate vertical element: 71,250 / f(MHz) mm — add 10% extra for trimming
  3. Calculate 4 radials: same length as vertical element
☐ Chosen frequency: _____ MHz | ☐ Element length: _____ mm | ☐ Radial length: _____ mm
STEP 2

Assemble the antenna

  1. Solder vertical element to centre pin of SO-239 (or N-type) connector
  2. Solder 4 radials to connector body, equally spaced (90° apart)
  3. Angle radials at 45° below horizontal for 50 Ω feedpoint impedance
  4. Secure with cable ties or bend wire at the connector body
STEP 3

VNA measurement — initial

  1. SOL-calibrate VNA for your frequency range (±30% of target)
  2. Connect assembled antenna to VNA port
  3. Record: resonant frequency, SWR at resonance, feedpoint impedance
  4. Switch to Smith chart — note where impedance plots relative to centre
☐ Initial resonant freq: _____ MHz | ☐ SWR: _____ | ☐ Z = _____ + j_____ Ω
STEP 4

Trim and optimise

  1. Resonance too low → trim vertical element (and optionally radials by same proportion)
  2. Resonance too high → extend with additional wire
  3. SWR good but not at 50 Ω → adjust radial droop angle to shift impedance
  4. Target: SWR ≤ 1.5:1, R ≈ 50 Ω, X ≈ 0

Lesson 12 Summary
  • A monopole + ground plane = dipole via image theory. Impedance = half a dipole: ~36 Ω
  • Quarter-wave element length (mm) = 71,250 / f(MHz) — same as one dipole arm
  • Radials provide the return current path — quality determines efficiency
  • More radials = lower ground loss resistance = better efficiency
  • Droop radials 45° downward to raise feedpoint impedance from 36 Ω to ~50 Ω
  • Elevated verticals (VHF/UHF): 4–8 radials sufficient. Ground-mounted HF: 32+ radials
