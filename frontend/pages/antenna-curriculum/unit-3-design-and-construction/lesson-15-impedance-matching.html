L-networks, Smith chart matching strategy, baluns, stub matching — and the Unit 3 capstone

CHAPTER 02

Why Matching Matters: Maximum Power Transfer

💡
The Big Idea: We've spent this unit building antennas that don't always present 50 Ω to the feedline — a Yagi driven element at 20 Ω, a monopole at 36 Ω, an EFHW at 2500 Ω. Impedance matching transforms one impedance to another, enabling maximum power transfer without reflections.

Maximum power transfer occurs when the source impedance (transmitter/feedline at 50 Ω) equals the load impedance (antenna). When they don't match:

  • Power reflects back toward the transmitter (quantified as SWR and return loss)
  • Feedline losses increase due to standing waves
  • Transmitter output stages may reduce power or trip protection

A matching network sits between feedline and antenna, transforming the antenna's actual impedance to appear as 50 Ω to the transmitter. Done well, the matching network introduces minimal loss and provides a broadband match. Done poorly, it introduces significant loss and narrows bandwidth.

CHAPTER 03

L-Network Matching

The L-network is the simplest matching network: just two reactive components (inductors and/or capacitors) arranged in an L-shape. It can match any real impedance to any other real impedance.

Design Procedure

Given: R_source (usually 50 Ω) and R_load (antenna resistance at resonance)

Step 1: Q factor
  Q = √(R_high / R_low − 1)

  where R_high = whichever of R_source, R_load is larger
        R_low  = whichever is smaller

Step 2: Shunt element reactance (in parallel with high-impedance side)
  X_shunt = R_high / Q

Step 3: Series element reactance (in series with low-impedance side)
  X_series = Q × R_low

Step 4: Calculate component values
  For an inductor:  L = X / (2π × f)    [Henries]
  For a capacitor:  C = 1 / (2π × f × X) [Farads]

Worked Example: Match 20 Ω Yagi to 50 Ω Coax at 145 MHz

R_high = 50 Ω (source), R_low = 20 Ω (load)
Q = √(50/20 − 1) = √(1.5) = 1.225

X_shunt (across 50 Ω side) = 50 / 1.225 = 40.8 Ω
X_series (in series with 20 Ω load) = 1.225 × 20 = 24.5 Ω

At 145 MHz:
  Shunt inductor: L = 40.8 / (2π × 145×10⁶) = 44.8 nH
  Series capacitor: C = 1 / (2π × 145×10⁶ × 24.5) = 44.8 pF

(Or invert: shunt capacitor and series inductor — both configurations work)
🔧
Interactive (future): L-network calculator with Smith chart path visualisation — enter source and load impedances, see the L-network component values and the matching path on a Smith chart.
⚠️
L-networks only match real impedances. If the antenna has significant reactance (X ≠ 0), first bring it to resonance (trim the element) or add a series/parallel element to cancel the reactance, then apply the L-network for the resistive mismatch.
CHAPTER 04

Smith Chart Matching: Visual Strategy

The Smith chart isn't just for reading impedance — it's a matching tool. Adding a lumped component moves the impedance point along a predictable path on the chart:

ActionMovement on Smith Chart
Add series inductor (+ jX)Move clockwise along constant-resistance circle (upward)
Add series capacitor (− jX)Move counter-clockwise along constant-resistance circle (downward)
Add shunt inductor (+ jB)Move counter-clockwise along constant-conductance circle
Add shunt capacitor (− jB)Move clockwise along constant-conductance circle
Add series transmission lineRotate clockwise around the chart at constant radius

Matching Strategy

  1. Plot the antenna load impedance ZL on the Smith chart
  2. Goal: reach the chart centre (50 Ω, Z = 1 + j0)
  3. Design a two-step path using the movements above
  4. Step 1 typically moves the point to the constant-resistance circle passing through the centre
  5. Step 2 moves along that circle to the centre
  6. Each step corresponds to one component — two components = L-network

This visual approach makes it obvious which component type to add at each step, and prevents algebraic errors. VNA software like NanoVNA-Saver and Rigol's PC software include Smith chart matching assistants.

CHAPTER 05

Baluns: Balanced to Unbalanced Conversion

A balun (Balanced-Unbalanced) solves the mismatch between balanced antennas (dipoles, folded dipoles — symmetric, no ground reference) and unbalanced feedlines (coax — one conductor is grounded).

The Problem Without a Balun

Without a balun, the coax braid becomes part of the antenna. Common-mode current flows down the outside of the braid, radiating and receiving from the feedline rather than the antenna. This:

  • Distorts the radiation pattern (radiation comes from the cable, not the antenna)
  • Couples RF into the shack (RFI on audio, computer interference)
  • Makes SWR measurements unreliable (the VNA measures the antenna + feedline)

1:1 Choke Balun (Current Balun)

Does not change impedance — presents high impedance to common-mode current while passing differential (balanced) current unchanged. The most common type for dipoles.

Construction: Wind 8–12 turns of coax through a ferrite toroid
  Suitable ferrite: FT-240-31 or FT-240-43 for HF
                    FT-82-61 or FT-50-61 for VHF/UHF
  Winding: coil the coax into the toroid with tight, even turns
  Result: presents >1000 Ω common-mode impedance at target frequency

4:1 Voltage Balun (Transformer Balun)

Transforms impedance 4:1 while converting balanced to unbalanced. Used when you need both impedance transformation and balance — for example, feeding a 200 Ω balanced folded dipole with 50 Ω coax.

Balun TypeRatioUse CaseKey Property
Choke (current) balun1:1Dipole → 50 Ω coaxStops common-mode; doesn't transform Z
Voltage (transformer) balun4:1200 Ω folded dipole → 50 ΩTransforms Z by 4× AND converts balanced/unbalanced
EFHW transformer49:1~2500 Ω EFHW → 50 ΩVery high impedance transformation for end-fed antennas
CHAPTER 06

When You Need a Balun

✅
Always use a 1:1 choke balun when:
  • Feeding a centre-fed dipole with coax
  • Feeding any balanced antenna (folded dipole, loop) with unbalanced coax
  • You observe RF feedback in the shack when transmitting
  • SWR on the rig doesn't match the VNA reading (feedline radiation)
🔑
Baluns are NOT needed for:
  • Quarter-wave ground plane verticals (unbalanced by design)
  • Yagi antennas with a gamma match (gamma match is inherently unbalanced)
  • Any antenna that is already unbalanced (one arm grounded)
CHAPTER 07

Stub Matching for Advanced Applications

A transmission line stub is a short section of feedline, open or short-circuited at one end, connected in parallel with the feedpoint. It presents a specific reactance that cancels the antenna's reactance, leaving only resistance to be matched.

Why Stubs Are Useful

  • No physical components (no inductors or capacitors to source, wind, or tune)
  • Low loss — coax has lower loss than lumped inductors at UHF
  • Can be built from surplus coax
  • Ideal for VHF and UHF matching where lumped components become impractical

Shorted vs Open Stubs

Stub TypeLength < λ/4Length = λ/4Length > λ/4
Short-circuitedInductive (+jX)Open circuit (high Z)Capacitive (−jX)
Open-circuitedCapacitive (−jX)Short circuit (low Z)Inductive (+jX)

By choosing stub length and type, you can add any desired reactance to cancel the antenna's reactance — leaving a pure resistance for the main matching network or direct coax connection.

🔧
Smith chart use: Plot the antenna impedance, then add a stub by rotating along a constant-conductance circle until you reach the real axis. The arc length corresponds to the electrical stub length.
CHAPTER 08

Lab: Matching and Balun Construction

LAB A

Measure a mismatched antenna, design and build an L-network

  1. Choose an antenna that presents a known mismatch at your target frequency — the Lesson 13 Yagi driven element (~20 Ω) works well, or a shortened whip
  2. VNA-measure the antenna: record R and X at target frequency
  3. If X ≠ 0, note its sign — cancel it with a series component first (series capacitor for inductive, series inductor for capacitive)
  4. Apply L-network design formula to match R_antenna to 50 Ω
  5. Build the L-network on a small piece of PCB or tag strip using appropriate component values
  6. Re-measure with VNA — compare SWR before and after network

LAB B

Wind a 1:1 choke balun and test on dipole

  1. Obtain a suitable ferrite toroid (FT-82-61 or similar for VHF)
  2. Wind 8–12 turns of RG-58 or RG-174 coax through the toroid core
  3. Keep turns tight and even; avoid gaps between turns
  4. Connect dipole from Lesson 11 first without balun — measure SWR, note any anomalies
  5. Insert balun between coax and dipole feedpoint — re-measure SWR
  6. Look for changes in resonant frequency or impedance — a change indicates feedline was radiating before

Lesson 15 Summary
  • Impedance matching enables maximum power transfer — transforms antenna Z to appear as 50 Ω
  • L-network: Q = √(R_high/R_low − 1) → two components, shunt and series
  • Smith chart: series L/C moves along resistance circles; shunt L/C moves along conductance circles
  • 1:1 choke balun: stops common-mode current on coax — required for all dipoles
  • 4:1 voltage balun: transforms impedance 4× AND converts balanced/unbalanced
  • Stub matching: transmission-line sections replace lumped components — lower loss at UHF
  • Always VNA-verify matching networks before declaring success
CHAPTER 10

🏆 Unit 3 Capstone: Design-Build-Measure Project

You've completed Unit 3 — Design and Construction. Now demonstrate the full cycle.

Your Project

Design an antenna for a frequency of your choosing. Complete the entire design-build-measure-iterate cycle:

  • Model in software (4nec2 or MMANA-GAL) — predict gain, impedance, pattern
  • Build it physically — document materials, dimensions, construction method
  • VNA-measure — S11, SWR, Smith chart trace, resonant frequency, bandwidth
  • Compare model to measurement — table of predicted vs actual for each parameter
  • Iterate — trim, adjust, add matching until SWR < 1.5:1 at target frequency
  • Write up the complete design cycle — include before/after VNA screenshots and annotated Smith chart
  • Publish as a build guide on RF-Hub — include all measurements so others can replicate

Suggested Projects

  • Half-wave dipole for any HF band (e.g., 40m, 20m, 10m)
  • Quarter-wave vertical with 4-radial ground plane for 433 MHz or 868 MHz
  • 3-element Yagi for 145 MHz or 440 MHz
  • Portable EFHW with 49:1 transformer for 40m/20m dual-band
  • J-pole for 145 MHz (stub matching built in)
