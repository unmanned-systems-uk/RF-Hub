CHAPTER 02

Half-Wave Dipole Physics

💡
The Big Idea: In Unit 1 we learned that a half-wave dipole is the reference antenna — 2.15 dBi gain, the yardstick against which everything is measured. Now we build one. This requires moving from theory to the real world, where velocity factors, wire diameter, and mounting effects all shift the calculated dimensions.

The Design Formula

A theoretical half-wave dipole in free space has a total length of λ/2 = 150/f(MHz) metres. Real wire antennas are shorter than this due to end effects and the velocity factor of the conductor:

Total dipole length (metres) = 142.5 / f(MHz)
Each arm length   (metres) =  71.25 / f(MHz)

Practical shortcut:
  Total (mm) = 142,500 / f(MHz)
  Each arm (mm) = 71,250 / f(MHz)

Derived from: L = (λ/2) × VF = (300/f × 0.5) × 0.95 = 142.5/f
📐
For 145 MHz (2m band): Each arm = 71,250 / 145 = 491 mm ≈ 49.1 cm. Total dipole = 982 mm ≈ 98.2 cm. That's why it's called "the 2-metre band" — the wavelength is ~2 m and a full-size dipole is just under 1 m.

Why Real Dipoles Are Shorter Than λ/2

Two effects conspire to shorten the required physical length:

  • End capacitance: The tips of a real wire have capacitance to their surroundings. This makes the antenna electrically longer than its physical length — so we shorten the wire to compensate. Typically accounts for 2–5% reduction.
  • Velocity factor: Current travels slightly slower than the speed of light in any real conductor (≈0.95c for bare wire). The wavelength in the conductor is shorter, so the antenna needs to be shorter. Typically accounts for another 5%.

Effect of Wire Diameter

Thicker elements lower the Q — this is why aluminium tube Yagi elements are wider-bandwidth than wire dipoles. The trade-off is weight and cost.

Current and Voltage Distribution

This builds directly on Lesson 2, but with practical consequences for construction:

⚠️
Insulate the tips: The high voltage at the dipole tips means insulators at the ends are essential, not optional. RF voltage at the tips of a 100W HF dipole can reach hundreds of volts — enough to arc through a damp rope or arc to a nearby mast.
CHAPTER 04

Impedance at Resonance

The feedpoint impedance of a half-wave dipole is not a fixed number — it depends significantly on the environment:

ConditionFeedpoint ImpedanceNotes
Free space (theoretical)73 + j0 ΩNo ground effects
Height = λ/2 above ground~65 ΩClose to 50 Ω — good for coax
Height = λ/4 above ground~90 ΩNeeds matching for 50 Ω coax
Inverted-V (120° included angle)~50–60 ΩNaturally closer to 50 Ω — convenient
Below resonanceR − jX (capacitive)Too short — trim less, or use loading
Above resonanceR + jX (inductive)Too long — shorten wire

In practice, a horizontal dipole at λ/2 height feeds reasonably directly with 50 Ω coax (with a balun). An inverted-V configuration is even more convenient — the drooping arms naturally bring impedance closer to 50 Ω while also fitting in a smaller space.

CHAPTER 05

Feed Methods

Centre-Fed (Standard)

Feed at the centre low-impedance point with coax. Simple and effective. However, a dipole is a balanced antenna (symmetric, with current distributed symmetrically) while coax is unbalanced (one conductor is grounded/shielded). Without a balun, shield current flows on the outside of the coax braid, distorting the radiation pattern and potentially causing RF interference.

🔑
Always use a balun when feeding a dipole with coax. A 1:1 choke balun (ferrite sleeve, or coiled coax) stops common-mode current from flowing back down the feedline. This is covered in depth in Lesson 15.

Off-Centre Fed Dipole (OCFD)

Feed at a point roughly 1/3 from one end rather than the centre. At this point the impedance is higher (~200 Ω), requiring a 4:1 balun to match 50 Ω coax. The benefit: an OCFD resonant on a fundamental frequency also has reasonable SWR at 3× and sometimes 7× that frequency, giving multiband operation from a single wire. Popular for HF operators wanting 40m, 20m, and 15m from one antenna.

Inverted-V

The centre is elevated (on a mast or pole) and the arms droop down at an angle. Advantages: single support needed, lower impedance than flat horizontal, smaller footprint, omnidirectional pattern (horizontal dipole has figure-8 pattern; inverted-V is more omnidirectional). Slight gain reduction vs horizontal. Excellent first antenna for limited-space HF operators.

CHAPTER 06

Bandwidth Considerations

Fan Dipole

Multiple dipole elements connected at the same feedpoint, each cut for a different band. When RF is applied, only the resonant dipole is "active" — the others present high impedance and don't radiate significantly. Result: multiband coverage with a single feedpoint, no tuner needed. Each pair of arms must be angled slightly apart so they don't couple to each other.

Trapped Dipole

Parallel LC circuits (traps) inserted at specific points along the wire. At the trap's resonant frequency, the trap presents very high impedance, electrically shortening the antenna. Below the trap frequency, the full length (including the outer section) is active. Allows a single wire to cover multiple bands — but traps add loss and require careful construction.

CHAPTER 07

Hands-On Lab: Build a 145 MHz Dipole

⚠️
Safety: This lab involves cutting wire and soldering. Work in a ventilated area. Ensure the VNA is disconnected from any transmitter before connecting your antenna. Do not transmit RF during VNA measurements.
STEP 1

Calculate dimensions

  1. Target frequency: 145 MHz (2m FM calling frequency)
  2. Each arm = 71,250 / 145 = 491 mm (cut slightly long — start at 500 mm)
  3. Material: copper wire (any gauge 0.5–2 mm) or thin copper pipe
  4. Record your calculated target length before cutting
☐ Calculated arm length: _____ mm | ☐ Actual cut length: _____ mm
STEP 2

Prepare materials

  1. Cut two equal wire lengths (your calculated arm + 20 mm extra for connection)
  2. Prepare 50 Ω coax: strip 20 mm of outer jacket, separate braid and centre conductor
  3. Connect one arm to the coax braid, one arm to the centre conductor
  4. Solder cleanly — cold joints introduce loss resistance and give false VNA readings
  5. Optionally: install a 1:1 choke balun (10+ turns of coax through a ferrite toroid) at the feedpoint
STEP 3

Mount and measure — initial

  1. Mount the dipole horizontally with arms level, at least 1 m above any metal surface
  2. Connect to calibrated VNA (SOL-calibrate first as per Lesson 10)
  3. Set sweep range: 130–160 MHz
  4. Find the SWR minimum — this is your actual resonant frequency
  5. Record: resonant frequency, SWR at resonance, impedance (R + jX)
☐ Initial resonant freq: _____ MHz | ☐ Initial SWR: _____ | ☐ Impedance: _____ + j_____ Ω
STEP 4

Trim to optimise

  1. If resonance is below 145 MHz → arms are too long → trim equally from both tips (5 mm at a time)
  2. If resonance is above 145 MHz → arms are too short → add wire or solder extensions
  3. Re-measure after each trim
  4. Target: resonance at 145 MHz with SWR ≤ 1.5:1 and R close to 50–73 Ω
  5. If R is correct but SWR is still high, reactance X ≠ 0 — keep trimming
☐ Final arm length: _____ mm | ☐ Final resonant freq: _____ MHz | ☐ Final SWR: _____ ☐ Final impedance: R = _____ Ω, X = _____ Ω | ☐ SWR < 2:1 bandwidth: _____ MHz
STEP 5

Document and compare

  1. Screenshot Smith chart and SWR plot — before and after trimming
  2. Calculate shortening percentage: (theoretical 491 mm − final length) / 491 mm × 100
  3. Note any discrepancy between theory and measurement — record your environment (height above ground, nearby structures)
  4. Save measurements to your RF-Hub equipment log

Lesson 11 Summary
  • Dipole arm length (mm) = 71,250 / f(MHz) — about 5% shorter than theoretical λ/4
  • Current: max at centre, zero at tips. Voltage: zero at centre, max at tips
  • Free-space impedance ~73 Ω; height and geometry affect actual feedpoint impedance
  • Always use a 1:1 balun when feeding a dipole with coax
  • Thicker wire = wider bandwidth; inverted-V = more compact with natural impedance reduction
  • Build-measure-trim cycle: cut long, measure, trim to target frequency
