Yagi-Uda Antennas: Adding Gain

Parasitic elements, reflectors, directors, feed methods, and building a 3-element 145 MHz Yagi

CHAPTER 02

Parasitic Coupling: How Non-Driven Elements Shape the Pattern

💡
The Big Idea: The Yagi-Uda antenna adds gain without adding feedlines. Extra conductive elements — not connected to the transmitter — are placed near the driven dipole. These parasitic elements re-radiate the energy they receive, and by carefully choosing their length and spacing, the re-radiated energy adds constructively in one direction and destructively in others. The result: a directional beam from passive conductors.

When a parasitic element is near a driven antenna, mutual coupling induces current in the parasitic. Whether that current leads or lags the driven current — and by how much — depends on the parasitic's length relative to resonance:

  • Parasitic element longer than resonant length → acts inductive → re-radiates with a phase lag → acts as a reflector
  • Parasitic element shorter than resonant length → acts capacitive → re-radiates with a phase lead → acts as a director

By placing a reflector behind the driven element and one or more directors in front, the re-radiated fields add in the forward direction and cancel in the rearward direction — creating a forward-directed beam.

Only one reflector is ever used. Adding a second reflector behind the first provides negligible additional gain. The diminishing-returns pattern means all design effort focuses on the number of directors.

CHAPTER 05

The Gain-Bandwidth Trade-Off

Adding more directors increases gain but narrows bandwidth — this is a fundamental physical relationship, not an engineering choice:

  • A 3-element Yagi for 145 MHz might have SWR < 2:1 bandwidth of 10–15 MHz
  • A 10-element Yagi optimised for maximum gain might only have 3–5 MHz bandwidth
  • A 10-element Yagi optimised for broadband might have 8–10 MHz bandwidth but 1–2 dB less gain
⚠️
Narrow-beam Yagis need accurate aiming. A 10-element Yagi with 3° half-power beamwidth must be pointed within ±1.5° of the target. An EME (moonbounce) station with a 20-element array must track the moon. More gain = more pointing accuracy required.
CHAPTER 06

Feed Methods

The driven element of a Yagi presents low impedance (typically 15–25 Ω for a 3-element design) rather than the 73 Ω of a free-space dipole — the parasitic elements lower it. A matching network is needed to feed 50 Ω coax.

Gamma Match

A small rod tapped onto one arm of the driven element at a calculated point, connected to the coax centre. Adjustable — good for optimising on the bench. The non-symmetrical design means a balun is advisable.

Hairpin Match (Beta Match)

A short shorted transmission-line stub connected across the feedpoint. Tunable by adjusting the stub length. Provides matching and is symmetrical — no separate balun needed if the driven element is split.

Folded Dipole

The driven element is a folded dipole (4× impedance of a regular dipole). Raises feedpoint Z to ~75–200 Ω range, closer to direct 50/75 Ω feed. Also wider bandwidth than a simple dipole element.

CHAPTER 07

Modelling in 4nec2 or MMANA-GAL

Before building, model the Yagi in free NEC-based antenna modelling software. This predicts gain, pattern, and feedpoint impedance — and reveals any design errors before you cut metal.

4nec2 (Windows, free)

  • Download from 4nec2.nl
  • Define elements as wire segments: start coordinate, end coordinate, diameter, number of segments
  • Set a voltage source on the driven element
  • Run frequency sweep to find resonance and impedance
  • Run far-field pattern to see gain and radiation pattern

MMANA-GAL (alternative, simpler UI)

  • Graphical element entry — easier for beginners than 4nec2's text-based input
  • Real-time pattern update as you adjust elements
  • Good for initial design exploration; 4nec2 for final validation

Starting Dimensions for 3-Element 145 MHz Yagi

Driven element: 975 mm total (487.5 mm each arm) — slightly short for matching
Reflector:      1030 mm total — ~5% longer than driven (mounted 0.2λ = 414 mm behind driven)
Director 1:     920 mm total — ~5% shorter (mounted 0.15λ = 310 mm in front of driven)

Element diameter: 6 mm aluminium tube (affects velocity factor slightly)
Boom: non-conducting (fibreglass) or insulate metallic boom from elements
📐
These are starting dimensions for a gamma-matched design. Model first, then build. Actual optimal dimensions depend on element diameter, boom material, and desired gain/bandwidth balance.
CHAPTER 08

Lab: Design, Model, Build a 3-Element 145 MHz Yagi

STEP 1

Model in 4nec2 or MMANA-GAL

  1. Enter the starting dimensions from Chapter 07 into your chosen software
  2. Set frequency: 144–146 MHz sweep
  3. Run simulation — record predicted gain, F/B ratio, feedpoint impedance
  4. Optimise: adjust element lengths and spacing to minimise SWR and maximise gain at 145 MHz
☐ Modelled gain: _____ dBi | ☐ F/B ratio: _____ dB | ☐ Feedpoint Z: _____ Ω ☐ Driven element final length: _____ mm | ☐ Reflector: _____ mm | ☐ Director: _____ mm
STEP 2

Build the physical Yagi

  1. Cut aluminium (or copper) elements to modelled lengths — leave 5 mm extra on each for trimming
  2. Mark boom at element positions: reflector, driven element, director
  3. Mount parasitic elements directly to boom (if non-conductive) or through insulating standoffs (if metal boom)
  4. Mount driven element with centre gap — prepare gamma match or feed accordingly
  5. Attach coax to feedpoint with appropriate matching and strain relief
STEP 3

VNA measurement and comparison

  1. SOL-calibrate VNA for 130–160 MHz range
  2. Connect Yagi feedpoint coax to VNA port
  3. Record S11, SWR, impedance, Smith chart trace
  4. Compare to model predictions — note any deviations
  5. Trim driven element (and adjust gamma match) to achieve SWR ≤ 1.5:1 at 145 MHz
☐ Measured resonant freq: _____ MHz | ☐ Measured SWR: _____ | ☐ Measured Z: _____ ☐ Model vs measured gain difference: _____ dB (requires field comparison test)
STEP 4

Compare with dipole from Lesson 11

  1. Point the Yagi toward a distant beacon, repeater, or SDR signal source
  2. Note signal strength (in SDR software or radio S-meter) with Yagi in peak direction
  3. Swap antenna for the Lesson 11 dipole — note signal strength
  4. Difference = practical gain of Yagi vs dipole
  5. Compare measured gain to modelled gain prediction

Lesson 13 Summary
  • Parasitic elements use mutual coupling to shape radiation — no direct electrical connection
  • Reflector: ~5% longer, ~0.2λ behind driven → reflects energy forward
  • Directors: ~5% shorter, ~0.15λ spacing → focus energy forward
  • More directors = more gain, narrower bandwidth, requires more precise aiming
  • Feedpoint impedance is reduced by parasitic coupling — needs matching (gamma, hairpin, or folded dipole)
  • Always model before building: 4nec2 or MMANA-GAL predict gain and impedance
  • VNA measurement + practical signal comparison validates the build
