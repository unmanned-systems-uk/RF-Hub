CHAPTER 02

Frequency-Independent Antenna Principles: Angles, Not Lengths

💡
The Big Idea: A resonant antenna (dipole, Yagi) depends on a specific physical length matching the wavelength. Change frequency significantly and the match degrades. Broadband antennas overcome this by using shapes defined by angles rather than lengths — the same angular geometry works at any frequency within a wide range.

The key insight was formalised by V.H. Rumsey in the 1950s: if an antenna's shape can be specified entirely by angles, with no characteristic length, then its properties are frequency-independent.

In practice, pure frequency-independence is impossible (any real antenna has finite size), but the principle guides designs that achieve 10:1 or greater bandwidth ratios:

  • A perfect cone angle doesn't change as frequency changes — neither does a discone's cone angle
  • A logarithmic spiral grows self-similarly at any scale — the active region just shifts position
  • An LPDA uses progressively scaled elements — the active region shifts along the array
CHAPTER 03

The Discone: How It Works, Bandwidth, Gain

Your Diamond D130N is a discone. It consists of:

  • A disc (or set of radials approximating a disc) at the top — fed at its centre
  • A cone (or set of drooping radials approximating a cone) below — connected to the coax braid

Why It Works Across Such a Wide Range

The discone is essentially a wideband vertical monopole. The disc acts as the active radiating element; the cone provides the ground plane. The angle of the cone (typically 25–30°) determines the characteristic impedance of the structure — and this angle doesn't change with frequency.

At any given frequency, the portion of the structure that is electrically active changes, but the same angular geometry continues to present a reasonable match to 50 Ω across the band.

The discone's gain is modest — typically 0–2 dBi. The gain-size-bandwidth trade-off (from Lesson 4) applies: to achieve 52:1 bandwidth, gain must be sacrificed. You can't have both wide bandwidth AND high gain in a small antenna.

CHAPTER 04

Log-Periodic Dipole Array (LPDA): The Yagi's Broadband Cousin

An LPDA looks similar to a Yagi — a boom with multiple dipole elements — but works on a completely different principle. In a Yagi, only one element is driven (the others are parasitic). In an LPDA, all elements are driven, connected in a phase-reversed transmission line.

How the LPDA Achieves Broadband Operation

The elements are scaled by a constant ratio τ (tau, typically 0.9–0.95):

τ = L₂/L₁ = L₃/L₂ = ... (ratio of adjacent element lengths)
σ = spacing ratio (similar constant: S₂/L₂ = constant)

At any given frequency, only the elements near resonance at that frequency
contribute significantly to radiation. As frequency changes, the "active region"
moves along the array — shorter elements at higher frequencies, longer at lower.

CHAPTER 05

Spiral Antennas: Inherently Circular Polarised and Broadband

A spiral antenna is wire or strip conductor wound in an Archimedean or equiangular spiral. It combines two rare properties simultaneously:

Inherently Broadband

The equiangular spiral's shape is defined by angles only — frequency-independent by Rumsey's principle. Practical spirals achieve 10:1 to 40:1 bandwidth.

Inherently Circular Polarised

The spiral geometry generates equal E-field components in both planes, 90° out of phase — exactly the condition for circular polarisation. Unlike crossed dipoles or helix, no special feed network is needed.

Spiral antennas are used in direction finding (DF) receivers, electronic warfare, and measurement systems where you need broadband, circularly polarised reception without knowing the signal's polarisation in advance.

SWR across bandVery low at resonance, rises steeply off-bandModerate (2–3:1) but consistent

Typical SDR applicationOptimised for specific mode/bandGeneral scanning, unknown signals
💡
Practical rule: Start with your D130N discone for general exploration and signal hunting. Once you identify an interesting signal on a specific frequency, build a resonant antenna (dipole or Yagi) for that frequency to extract maximum performance.

Lesson 14 Summary
  • Broadband antennas use angle-defined geometry (not lengths) — from Rumsey's frequency-independence principle
  • Discone (D130N): 25–1300 MHz, 0–2 dBi, omnidirectional vertical — ideal for scanning
  • LPDA: all elements driven, active region shifts with frequency, 6–9 dBi, directional — good for wideband directional monitoring
  • Spiral: inherently broadband AND circularly polarised — rare combination for DF and EW applications
  • Broadband = lower peak gain than resonant — the fundamental trade-off
  • Use broadband for unknown frequencies; resonant for known, optimised performance
