Cellular Generations
The evolution from voice-only 2G through broadband 5G
2G
GSMGlobal System for Mobile Communications
The first fully digital cellular standard (1991). Replaced analogue 1G systems. Uses TDMA/FDMA access. Operates on 900 MHz and 1800 MHz in the UK. Primarily voice and SMS. Still used as a fallback in many areas, though many UK operators have now switched it off.
9.6 kbps data
2.5G
GPRSGeneral Packet Radio Service
Packet-switched data overlay on GSM. Enables "always-on" IP connectivity by sharing unused GSM timeslots. Introduced WAP browsing and MMS. Multiple timeslots can be used simultaneously for higher throughput.
Up to 114 kbps
2.75G
EDGEEnhanced Data rates for GSM Evolution
Improved GPRS using 8PSK modulation instead of GMSK, tripling data rates. Sometimes called "E-GPRS". Still visible on phones as the "E" indicator. A software upgrade to existing GPRS infrastructure.
Up to 384 kbps
3G
UMTS / WCDMAUniversal Mobile Telecommunications System
First purpose-built mobile broadband standard. Uses CDMA spread-spectrum (W-CDMA variant) rather than TDMA. Operates primarily at 2100 MHz (Band 1) in the UK. Introduced video calling. The network behind "3" or "H" indicators.
Up to 2 Mbps
3.5G
HSPA / HSPA+High Speed Packet Access
Major UMTS enhancement. HSDPA (downlink) and HSUPA (uplink) use adaptive modulation, HARQ retransmission, and shorter frame intervals. HSPA+ added MIMO and 64QAM. Shown as "H" or "H+" on phones. Most 3G sites in the UK ran HSPA+.
Up to 168 Mbps (HSPA+)
4G
LTELong Term Evolution
A complete redesign. Uses OFDMA (downlink) and SC-FDMA (uplink). All-IP architecture — voice is carried as VoLTE (Voice over LTE). Flexible channel bandwidths (1.4–20 MHz). Defined by the 3GPP standards body. What your Diamond D130N and LTE test antenna are designed for.
Up to 300 Mbps (Cat 6)
4G+
LTE-AdvancedLTE-A / LTE-Advanced Pro
Builds on LTE with Carrier Aggregation (multiple bands simultaneously), 4×4 MIMO, 256QAM, and HetNet (heterogeneous network) support. LTE-A Pro ("4.5G") adds Massive MIMO (up to 64T64R), LAA (LTE in unlicensed spectrum), and early 5G concepts.
Up to 3 Gbps (theoretical)
5G
NRNew Radio
Current-generation standard. Two frequency ranges: FR1 (sub-6 GHz, including 700 MHz, 3.5 GHz) and FR2 (mmWave, 26/28/39 GHz). Flexible numerology (subcarrier spacings from 15 kHz to 240 kHz). Massive MIMO beamforming standard. NSA (Non-Standalone) mode anchors to 4G core initially.
Up to 20 Gbps (theoretical)

Why Does This Matter for RF Work?

Each generation uses different frequency bands, modulation schemes, and channel structures. When you're looking at a signal on your RSA5065N, knowing the generation tells you what kind of signal structure to expect — GSM signals look completely different to LTE signals on a spectrum analyser, even if they're at adjacent frequencies.

Network Architecture
The nodes, boxes, and connections that make up a cellular network
2G/3G Architecture (Legacy)
BTSBase Transceiver Station
The physical radio equipment at the base of a cell tower — the antennas, power amplifiers, transceivers, and feeder cables. This is the thing you actually see on masts. The BTS handles the radio interface directly with your phone. Each BTS covers one or more sectors.
BSCBase Station Controller
Controls a group of BTS units. Manages radio resources, handover decisions, and frequency allocation. Sits between the BTS and the MSC in the GSM architecture. Often co-located with the MSC at a central exchange building.
MSCMobile Switching Centre
The core switching node for voice calls in 2G/3G. Routes calls between mobile users and to the PSTN (Public Switched Telephone Network). Manages mobility — tracking which cells users are in. Contains or interfaces with the HLR and VLR.
HLRHome Location Register
The master database for all subscribers on a network. Stores your SIM's IMSI, subscribed services, and current location (which VLR you're registered with). When you call someone on another network, their HLR is queried to find them.
VLRVisitor Location Register
Temporary database at the MSC storing details of subscribers currently in that MSC's coverage area. When you move into a new area, your details are copied from the HLR to the local VLR — avoiding constant HLR lookups during calls.
RNCRadio Network Controller (3G)
The 3G equivalent of the BSC. Controls a group of Node B (3G base stations). Manages soft handover (a 3G-specific feature where a phone can be connected to multiple base stations simultaneously), power control, and radio bearers.
Node B3G Base Station
The 3G equivalent of the BTS. The physical radio equipment on a 3G cell site. Often co-located with 2G BTS equipment on the same mast. Connected to the RNC via the Iub interface.
4G LTE Architecture (EPC)
UE (Phone) ──── [Uu interface / LTE radio] ──── eNB (eNodeB) │ [S1-MME] [S1-U] │ │ MME S-GW (Control) (User data) │ P-GW ────── Internet / IMS │ │ HSS PCRF (Subscriber DB) (Policy)
eNodeBeNB · Evolved Node B
The LTE base station. Unlike 3G where the RNC handled most intelligence, the eNodeB is "smarter" — it manages radio resources, handover decisions, and scheduling autonomously. eNodeBs communicate with each other directly via the X2 interface, enabling faster handovers. What you see on modern masts alongside legacy equipment. 🔧 On CellMapper, eNodeB IDs appear as "eNB ID". The full Cell ID = eNB ID × 256 + Cell Index.
EPCEvolved Packet Core
The 4G core network. Entirely packet-switched — there is no circuit-switched voice path. Voice is delivered via VoLTE (IMS). The EPC comprises the MME, S-GW, P-GW, HSS, and PCRF nodes.
MMEMobility Management Entity
The control-plane node of the EPC. Handles authentication, security, session establishment, and tracking area updates. When your phone wakes up on LTE, it registers with the MME. The MME is the "control brain" — it never touches user data directly.
S-GWServing Gateway
Routes and forwards user data packets between the eNodeB and the P-GW. Acts as the local mobility anchor — when you handover between eNodeBs, the S-GW stays the same, simplifying the data path. The S-GW is the "local postman" for your data.
P-GWPDN Gateway
The gateway between the LTE network and the internet (PDN = Packet Data Network). Allocates your IP address, enforces QoS policies, and interfaces with external networks. The P-GW is where your data leaves the mobile network and enters the public internet.
HSSHome Subscriber Server
The 4G/5G equivalent of the HLR. Combined authentication centre and subscriber database. Stores authentication vectors, QoS profiles, and PDN connectivity settings. Interfaces with the MME for authentication.
VoLTEVoice over LTE
Voice calls carried as IP packets over LTE using the IMS (IP Multimedia Subsystem). Required dedicated QoS handling (QCI 1 bearer). Better voice quality than legacy circuit-switched calls. When operators switch off 2G/3G, VoLTE becomes the only voice option — which is why some older phones lose calling ability on 4G-only networks.
5G Architecture (5GC / Next Gen)
gNodeBgNB · 5G Base Station
The 5G base station. Handles the 5G NR radio interface. In NSA (Non-Standalone) mode, a gNB works alongside an existing eNB and shares the 4G core — the "master" eNB handles signalling while the gNB adds extra capacity. In SA (Standalone) mode, it connects directly to the 5G Core.
NSA vs SANon-Standalone vs Standalone
NSA (Option 3): 5G NR radio anchored to 4G LTE. Uses the existing 4G EPC core. Early 5G deployments in the UK are mostly NSA. Your phone shows "5G" but is still using the 4G control plane.

SA (Option 2): Full 5G with the 5G Core (5GC). Network slicing, ultra-low latency, and true 5G features only available in SA mode.
5GC5G Core
Service-Based Architecture (SBA). Replaces the EPC's point-to-point interfaces with a microservices model. Key functions include AMF (Access and Mobility Management — replaces MME), UPF (User Plane Function — replaces S/P-GW), and SMF (Session Management Function). Enables network slicing — running multiple virtual networks on one physical infrastructure.

Frequency & Channel Concepts
How spectrum is divided and assigned in cellular systems
FDDFrequency Division Duplex
Uplink and downlink use different frequencies simultaneously. Requires a frequency "guard band" (duplex gap) between UL and DL. Most cellular spectrum is FDD — for example, LTE Band 20 uses 832–862 MHz for uplink and 791–821 MHz for downlink, with a 30 MHz separation. Your phone transmits and receives at the same time on different frequencies. 📡 On your RSA5065N, FDD signals appear as two separate occupied spectrum blocks with a gap between them.
TDDTime Division Duplex
Uplink and downlink use the same frequency but at different times. No duplex gap needed — more efficient spectrum use. Common in unpaired spectrum allocations. LTE Band 38 (2600 MHz) and Band 40 (2300 MHz) are TDD. The UL/DL ratio can be dynamically adjusted. 5G NR uses TDD extensively, especially at higher frequencies. 📡 TDD signals appear as a single occupied block that "bursts" — you'll see activity at a regular duty cycle corresponding to the frame structure.
ARFCNAbsolute Radio Frequency Channel Number
A numerical index that unambiguously identifies a radio channel frequency in GSM. Instead of saying "935.2 MHz", GSM uses ARFCN numbers. ARFCN 1 = 935.2 MHz downlink / 890.2 MHz uplink (Band 900). Each ARFCN step = 200 kHz. Knowing an ARFCN lets you calculate the exact carrier frequency.
EARFCNE-UTRA Absolute Radio Frequency Channel Number
The LTE equivalent of ARFCN. A number that identifies the centre frequency of an LTE carrier. Each band has a range of valid EARFCNs. Formula: f = f_offset + 0.1 × (EARFCN − EARFCN_offset). For example, EARFCN 6300 = 2655 MHz (Band 7 downlink). 🔧 CellMapper and SDR tools like SDRangel display EARFCNs. Converting to MHz tells you the exact frequency to look for on your RSA5065N.
NR-ARFCN5G NR Channel Number
The 5G equivalent of EARFCN. Covers a much wider range than LTE to accommodate frequencies from 450 MHz to 52.6 GHz. Uses a finer 15 kHz global frequency raster for sub-6 GHz and coarser 60 kHz for mmWave.
Duplex GapGuard Band between UL and DL (FDD)
The unused spectrum between uplink and downlink in FDD systems. Required to prevent transmitter desensitisation — your phone's transmitter (uplink) must not bleed into its own receiver (downlink). The duplexer filter in the phone separates these. The gap size varies by band — Band 1 has a 190 MHz duplex gap, Band 20 has 41 MHz.
Carrier AggregationCA · LTE-A feature
Combining multiple LTE component carriers (CCs) to increase throughput. Can aggregate carriers in the same band (intra-band CA) or different bands (inter-band CA). Each CC can be up to 20 MHz. A "5CA" device can use up to 5 × 20 MHz = 100 MHz simultaneously. Shown as "4G+" or "LTE-A" on phones. 🔧 With CellMapper, you can often see which bands a specific tower is broadcasting — this tells you which CA combinations are possible from that site.
Channel BandwidthBW · Carrier bandwidth in MHz
LTE supports: 1.4, 3, 5, 10, 15, and 20 MHz channel bandwidths. The bandwidth determines how many Resource Blocks (RBs) are available: 1.4 MHz = 6 RBs, 20 MHz = 100 RBs. Wider bandwidth = more capacity. Most UK LTE deployments use 10 or 20 MHz. 📡 On your spectrum analyser, a 20 MHz LTE carrier looks like a flat-topped block approximately 18 MHz wide (with a small guard at each edge).
PCIPhysical Cell Identity
A number (0–503) that identifies a cell locally — not globally. Used by phones to distinguish between nearby cells on the same frequency. PCIs are reused across the network but must not repeat in adjacent cells (a "PCI collision"). Your phone synchronises to a cell using its PCI. Visible in engineering mode and on CellMapper. 🔧 SDR tools that can decode LTE (like LTE-Cell-Scanner) will display the PCI. Combined with EARFCN, you can identify a specific cell.

Multiple Access Methods
How multiple users share the same spectrum

The Core Problem

Hundreds of phones need to share the same spectrum simultaneously without stepping on each other. Multiple access schemes divide the available radio resource across users — in time, frequency, code, or space.

TDMATime Division Multiple Access · 2G GSM
Divides a frequency channel into time slots. Each GSM carrier (200 kHz wide) is divided into 8 time slots (a TDMA frame of ~4.6 ms). Each user is assigned one or more slots. Analogy: 8 people taking turns speaking on a single walkie-talkie channel, so fast it sounds simultaneous.
FDMAFrequency Division Multiple Access
Divides the spectrum into separate frequency channels, each assigned to a user. Used in 1G analogue systems and as the outer layer in GSM (where TDMA sits within each 200 kHz FDMA channel). Also the basis of OFDMA — just with very many narrowly spaced subcarriers.
CDMACode Division Multiple Access · 3G UMTS
All users share the same wideband frequency simultaneously, distinguished by unique spreading codes (Walsh codes). Each user's signal is spread across the full bandwidth — their signal appears as noise to others who don't have their code. The receiver uses the code to "de-spread" and extract the signal. Requires precise power control — all signals must arrive at the base station at equal power ("near-far problem").
OFDMAOrthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access · LTE/5G downlink
Divides the channel into hundreds of narrow, closely spaced subcarriers (15 kHz each in LTE). The subcarriers are orthogonal — they don't interfere even though they overlap. Different users are assigned different subcarriers within the same timeslot — flexible time-frequency resource allocation. Excellent resilience to multipath and frequency-selective fading. Used for downlink in LTE. 📡 OFDM signals have a distinctive flat-topped, sharp-edged spectrum shape — very different from CDMA or TDMA signals.
SC-FDMASingle Carrier FDMA · LTE uplink
Used for the LTE uplink (phone to tower). Similar to OFDMA but with lower Peak-to-Average Power Ratio (PAPR) — important because phones have limited battery power and amplifier headroom. SC-FDMA maintains frequency-domain scheduling flexibility while being kinder to power amplifiers. Also called "DFT-spread OFDMA".

Modulation Schemes
How bits are encoded onto radio waves — higher order = more data, needs better signal
GMSKGaussian Minimum Shift Keying · 2G GSM
GSM's modulation scheme. A form of frequency shift keying with Gaussian filtering to smooth transitions. 1 bit per symbol. Constant envelope (no amplitude variation) — great for power-efficient amplifiers. Very robust but low spectral efficiency. The "G" (Gaussian) refers to the pulse shaping filter that reduces bandwidth.
QPSKQuadrature Phase Shift Keying
4 phase states → 2 bits per symbol. Used in LTE for control channels and in poor signal conditions. Robust — requires lower SNR than higher-order modulation. When your phone drops to QPSK, throughput falls but connection is maintained. Also used in 3G (WCDMA uses QPSK on downlink).
16QAMQuadrature Amplitude Modulation
16 constellation points (4 amplitude × 4 phase) → 4 bits per symbol. Used in LTE when signal conditions are moderate. Requires better SNR than QPSK. The phone and base station continuously negotiate which MCS (Modulation and Coding Scheme) to use based on channel quality.
64QAM64-state QAM
64 constellation points → 6 bits per symbol. Used in LTE downlink when signal is strong. Requires significantly better SNR — constellation points are closer together. Peak LTE throughput figures (150 Mbps on 20 MHz) assume 64QAM with 2×2 MIMO.
256QAM256-state QAM · LTE-A, 5G
256 constellation points → 8 bits per symbol. Requires excellent SNR — only used close to the base station. Introduced in LTE-Advanced Pro and standard in 5G. 33% more capacity than 64QAM. Requires high-quality amplifiers and filters to maintain linearity.
MCSModulation and Coding Scheme
A combined indicator of modulation order and channel coding rate. The base station selects the MCS for each user based on the CQI (Channel Quality Indicator) reported by the phone. Higher MCS = higher throughput but needs better signal. LTE has 28 MCS indices (0–27). This is Adaptive Modulation and Coding (AMC) in action.

Adaptive Modulation in Practice

As you move away from a tower, your phone's signal gets weaker. The network adapts the modulation scheme automatically: close to the tower → 256QAM; moderate distance → 64QAM or 16QAM; poor signal → QPSK. This is why throughput drops gradually as you move away, rather than the connection cutting off abruptly. Your RSA5065N can't decode the data, but you can observe the signal power — the modulation change happens transparently within the same occupied bandwidth.

LTE Physical Layer Structure
How LTE organises time and frequency into transmittable chunks

The LTE Time-Frequency Grid

LTE organises its radio resource as a 2D grid: frequency on one axis (subcarriers, 15 kHz spacing) and time on the other (OFDM symbols, slots, subframes, frames). Resources are allocated to users in chunks called Resource Blocks.

Subcarrier15 kHz spacing in LTE
The basic frequency unit in LTE OFDM. Each subcarrier is 15 kHz wide. A 20 MHz LTE channel contains 1200 data subcarriers (plus guard subcarriers). The subcarriers are mutually orthogonal — their peaks align with each other's nulls, preventing interference between adjacent subcarriers.
Resource BlockRB · Scheduling unit
The smallest unit the scheduler can allocate to a user: 12 subcarriers × 1 slot (0.5 ms) = 84 resource elements. A 20 MHz LTE carrier has 100 RBs. The eNodeB scheduler assigns RBs to users every millisecond (1 TTI = 2 slots = 1 subframe). Think of RBs as the "pixels" of the LTE time-frequency grid.
TTITransmission Time Interval · 1 ms in LTE
The scheduling interval — how often the base station makes new resource allocation decisions. In LTE: 1 TTI = 1 subframe = 2 slots = 1 ms. This is why LTE feels much more responsive than 3G (where the TTI was 10 ms). 5G NR has configurable TTI depending on numerology.
Frame Structure10 ms frame · 1 ms subframe · 0.5 ms slot
LTE radio time hierarchy:
1 frame = 10 ms
1 frame = 10 subframes
1 subframe = 2 slots = 1 TTI = 1 ms
1 slot = 0.5 ms = 7 OFDM symbols (normal CP)
So 1 frame contains 140 OFDM symbols.
CPCyclic Prefix
A guard interval prepended to each OFDM symbol — a copy of the end of the symbol placed at its beginning. Absorbs multipath reflections (inter-symbol interference). Normal CP = 4.7 µs (handles reflections up to ~1.4 km path difference). Extended CP = 16.7 µs (for very large cells or high multipath environments).
CQIChannel Quality Indicator
A number (0–15) reported by the phone to the base station, indicating how good the received signal quality is. Higher CQI = better signal → higher MCS selected. CQI 0 means "out of range". CQI 15 means excellent conditions → 64QAM or 256QAM. The base station uses CQI reports to adapt scheduling and modulation.
HARQHybrid Automatic Repeat Request
LTE's retransmission mechanism. If a packet is received with errors, the receiver requests a retransmission but cleverly combines the error-containing received packet with the retransmission — increasing the chance of successful decoding. "Hybrid" because it combines FEC (Forward Error Correction) with ARQ retransmission. HARQ operates at the physical layer with very low latency (~8 ms round trip).
Numerologyµ · 5G NR concept
5G NR introduced flexible subcarrier spacings to handle different frequency ranges and use cases: µ=0 (15 kHz, same as LTE), µ=1 (30 kHz), µ=2 (60 kHz), µ=3 (120 kHz, mmWave), µ=4 (240 kHz, reference only). Higher numerology = shorter symbols = better for higher frequencies and low-latency applications, but more overhead.

Signal Measurements & Quality Metrics
What the numbers mean — and what counts as good, bad, or ugly

Important: RSSI ≠ RSRP ≠ Signal Quality

A common mistake is treating RSSI as "the signal". In LTE, RSRP is the key metric — it measures only the reference signals, not total received power (which includes interference). RSRQ and SINR tell you about quality. You need to understand all three to properly diagnose a connection.

RSRP vs RSSI Explained

RSSI is the total power received across the entire channel bandwidth — including signal, interference, and noise. RSRP measures only the power of the LTE reference signals (specific known pilot subcarriers). Because RSRP ignores interference, two cells with identical RSSI can have very different RSRP — the one with more interference has lower RSRP. RSRP is always lower than RSSI by approximately: RSRP ≈ RSSI − 10×log₁₀(12 × N_RB) for N_RB resource blocks.

Network Identifiers
The numbering systems that uniquely identify networks, cells, and subscribers
PLMNPublic Land Mobile Network
The unique identifier for a mobile operator's network, formed by combining MCC + MNC. For example, EE in the UK = 234-30. Your phone's SIM contains the home PLMN — when the phone searches for networks, it looks for matching PLMNs first (home network), then roaming partners.
MCCMobile Country Code
3-digit country identifier. UK = 234 (and 235 for some operators). Used in conjunction with MNC to form the PLMN. US = 310–316, Germany = 262, France = 208.
MNCMobile Network Code
2 or 3-digit operator identifier within a country. Combined with MCC to uniquely identify a network. See the UK Operators table below for UK MNCs.
IMSIInternational Mobile Subscriber Identity
The globally unique identifier stored on your SIM card. Format: MCC + MNC + MSIN (Mobile Subscriber Identification Number). 15 digits maximum. Never transmitted in plain text after initial registration — the TMSI (Temporary Mobile Subscriber Identity) is used instead to protect privacy. The IMSI is what cellular intercept systems attempt to capture (IMSI catchers / Stingrays).
IMEIInternational Mobile Equipment Identity
The unique identifier for the physical handset (not the SIM). 15 digits. Format: TAC (Type Allocation Code, 8 digits) + Serial Number (6 digits) + check digit. Used by operators to block stolen phones (CEIR/EIR — Equipment Identity Register). Unlike the IMSI, the IMEI is tied to the hardware, not the subscriber.
Cell IDCGI · Cell Global Identity
The globally unique identifier for a cell: MCC + MNC + LAC + Cell ID. In LTE, often expressed as eNB ID + Cell Index. CellMapper stores cells by this identifier. Used to map coverage and locate towers. 🔧 In LTE: Cell ID = eNB ID × 256 + sector number (0, 1, or 2 for 3-sector sites).
TACTracking Area Code · LTE equivalent of LAC
Groups a set of cells into a Tracking Area. When your phone is idle, the network only knows which Tracking Area it's in — not which specific cell. When a call arrives, the network pages all cells in the TA simultaneously. When you move to a new TA, your phone sends a Tracking Area Update (TAU) to the MME.
LACLocation Area Code · 2G/3G equivalent of TAC
The 2G/3G equivalent of Tracking Area Code. Groups cells for paging purposes. Location Area Updates (LAU) are sent when moving between LAs. Visible in CellMapper alongside Cell IDs for 2G/3G cells.
PCIPhysical Cell Identity · LTE radio-layer ID
Range 0–503. Determines the primary synchronisation signal (PSS) and secondary synchronisation signal (SSS) sequences that identify the cell at the radio layer. Phones use PSS/SSS to synchronise with and identify cells. PCIs are planned carefully to avoid confusion between neighbouring cells.

MVNO Note

MVNOs (Mobile Virtual Network Operators — giffgaff, Sky Mobile, Tesco Mobile, etc.) do not own spectrum. They use the infrastructure of a host MNO. On the radio layer, their signals are indistinguishable from the host network. CellMapper and RF tools will only see the host MNO's cells.

Antennas & MIMO in Cellular
How cellular systems use multiple antennas to multiply capacity and reliability
MIMOMultiple Input Multiple Output
Using multiple antennas at both the transmitter and receiver simultaneously. Denoted as M×N (e.g., 2×2, 4×4). "Input" = transmitter antennas, "Output" = receiver antennas. MIMO can be used for two purposes: Spatial Multiplexing (send multiple data streams simultaneously, multiplying throughput) or Diversity (send the same data multiple ways, improving reliability). LTE base stations are typically 2T2R or 4T4R; 5G Massive MIMO uses 64T64R or more.
Spatial MultiplexingThe "multiply throughput" MIMO mode
Sending independent data streams on each antenna, relying on multipath propagation to separate them at the receiver. Each stream is called a "layer". 2-layer (rank 2) transmission doubles peak throughput versus a single antenna. Requires sufficient spatial separation between antennas and rich multipath — works better indoors and in urban environments than open fields with clear LOS. 📡 The "rank" of the MIMO transmission (number of parallel streams) is indicated by the RI (Rank Indicator) reported by the phone.
Transmit DiversityThe "improve reliability" MIMO mode
Sending the same data from multiple antennas with different processing (SFBC — Space Frequency Block Coding). The receiver combines the copies coherently, gaining diversity against fading. Used for control channels (which must be reliable) and in poor channel conditions where spatial multiplexing is not beneficial.
Cross-Polarisation±45° dual-polarised antenna elements
Modern cellular antennas use two polarisations — typically +45° and −45° from vertical. This gives two orthogonal signal paths within the same physical antenna. The two paths are sufficiently decorrelated to support 2×2 MIMO from a single physical antenna panel. This is why modern LTE antennas look like a single slim panel but support MIMO. 🔧 When you see a modern "blade" antenna on a tower, it almost certainly contains cross-polarised elements supporting at minimum 2T2R MIMO.
BeamformingSteering signals toward specific users
Using an array of antenna elements with controlled phase and amplitude to focus radiated energy toward a specific user. Increases signal strength at the target without increasing total transmit power — effectively increasing SINR. Used extensively in 5G Massive MIMO. Analogue beamforming (one beam), Digital beamforming (many simultaneous beams).
Massive MIMOmMIMO · 5G hallmark technology
Base station antenna arrays with many elements — typically 64T64R or 128T128R. Enables simultaneous beams to dozens of users (MU-MIMO — Multi-User MIMO). The many antennas create very narrow, precise beams. Standard in 5G NR deployments — those rectangular panels you see on new 5G masts with many small elements are Massive MIMO arrays.
SectorisationDividing a cell site into directional sectors
Most tower sites use 3-sector layouts — three panel antennas each covering approximately 120° horizontally. This triples the capacity of an omnidirectional site. Urban areas may use 6 sectors. Rural sites sometimes use 2 sectors or omni. Each sector is a separate "cell" with its own Cell ID, PCI, and frequency allocation. 🔧 On CellMapper, a 3-sector site appears as 3 separate cell records (Cell IDs differing by 1 or 2 within the same eNodeB ID).
Electrical TiltRemote electrical tilt — adjusting coverage without climbing
Adjusting the downtilt of the antenna beam by shifting the phase relationships between antenna elements — electronically, without physically tilting the antenna. Allows operators to fine-tune coverage from the ground (or remotely). More downtilt = smaller coverage radius but better signal within it. RET (Remote Electrical Tilt) motorised actuators allow adjustment via O&M systems.

Coverage & Propagation Concepts
How signals travel, fade, and how networks plan for it
Path LossSignal attenuation with distance
The reduction in signal power as it travels through space. In free space: PL = 20×log₁₀(d) + 20×log₁₀(f) + 32.4 dB (d in km, f in MHz). Doubling distance adds ~6 dB of path loss. Higher frequencies lose power faster with distance — which is why 700 MHz covers more ground than 2600 MHz. Real-world path loss is much higher than free space due to terrain, buildings, and vegetation.
Link BudgetThe accounting of gains and losses in a radio link
A calculation that determines the maximum allowable path loss (MAPL) for a link: MAPL = Tx Power + Tx Gain − Tx Losses + Rx Gain − Rx Sensitivity − Fade Margin. If MAPL exceeds the actual path loss, the link works. Link budgets determine cell range during network planning. 📡 The S11 measurements you take with your Rigol VNA feed directly into link budget calculations — antenna gain and return loss (mismatch loss) are factors in the link budget.
HandoverHO · Switching between cells mid-connection
Moving an active connection from one cell to another as the user moves. LTE uses hard handover (X2 or S1 interface) — the connection switches cleanly. 3G WCDMA used soft handover — the phone was simultaneously connected to multiple cells, and the best received frame was selected. LTE cannot do soft handover due to OFDMA's orthogonality requirements.
NLOSNon-Line of Sight
Communication where there is no direct unobstructed path between antennas. Buildings, terrain, and vegetation cause diffraction, reflection, and attenuation. LTE (and all cellular systems) must work in NLOS — the network is planned assuming NLOS conditions. Higher frequencies (5G mmWave) struggle significantly with NLOS. 🔧 Your omnidirectional discone antenna test showed this — building penetration loss and multipath reflections are NLOS phenomena.
FadingShort-term signal variation due to multipath
Fast fading (Rayleigh): Rapid signal fluctuations as multipath reflections add constructively and destructively. Changes over fractions of a wavelength of movement. LTE combats this with OFDM's flat frequency response per subcarrier and HARQ retransmission.

Slow fading (shadowing): Large-scale variation as you pass behind buildings or hills. Log-normal distribution. Planned for using the fade margin in the link budget.
PagingNetwork calling an idle phone
When a call or data arrives for an idle phone, the network doesn't know exactly which cell it's in — only its Tracking Area (LTE) or Location Area (2G/3G). The network sends a paging message to all cells in that TA/LA simultaneously. The phone wakes up from idle, reads the paging channel, and if it sees its own TMSI, responds to initiate the connection.
Cell Breathing3G CDMA phenomenon — cell size changes with load
Unique to CDMA/WCDMA: as more users are added to a cell, the interference level rises, reducing the effective coverage radius — the cell "shrinks". A heavily loaded 3G cell may only cover half the area of a lightly loaded one. LTE (OFDMA) does not have this problem — cell size is independent of load.
ISDInter-Site Distance · Planning parameter
The distance between adjacent cell sites in a network. Determined by coverage and capacity requirements. Urban LTE: 200–500 m ISD. Suburban: 500 m–2 km. Rural: 2–15 km. 5G mmWave requires very small ISD (50–200 m) due to short range. ISD determines how many sites are needed to cover an area.

SDR & Practical Tools
Terminology relevant to observing cellular signals with software-defined radio and network analysis tools
PSS / SSSPrimary & Secondary Synchronisation Signals
LTE broadcast signals used for cell search. The PSS provides timing synchronisation within the frame and carries part of the PCI (0, 1, or 2). The SSS provides frame timing and the remainder of the PCI (0–167). Together they give the full PCI (0–503). SDR LTE decoders (LTE-Cell-Scanner, FALCON) use PSS/SSS to detect and identify cells without decoding data. 🔧 On a spectrum analyser, you can't directly see PSS/SSS — they're embedded within the LTE signal. But SDR software like LTE-Cell-Scanner can extract them from IQ data captured with your HackRF.
CRSCell-specific Reference Signal
Known pilot signals transmitted by the eNodeB on fixed resource elements throughout the LTE frame. Used by phones to estimate channel quality and perform coherent demodulation. Also what RSRP measures. CRS patterns are determined by PCI — different cells use different CRS locations to avoid interference. CRS overhead is 4–14% of total capacity.
MIB / SIBMaster Information Block / System Information Blocks
MIB: Broadcast on the PBCH (Physical Broadcast Channel). Contains essential parameters: bandwidth, frame number, PHICH configuration. Transmitted every 40 ms. The first thing a phone reads after synchronisation.

SIBs: System Information Blocks carry additional network parameters (neighbour cell lists, access restrictions, paging configuration). SIB1 contains PLMN and TAC identifiers — from here you can identify the operator and tracking area without having a SIM.
PBCHPhysical Broadcast Channel
Carries the MIB. Transmitted in the centre 6 RBs of the carrier (72 subcarriers) on a specific set of OFDM symbols. This "centre 6 RB" structure means you can detect and partially decode an LTE carrier without knowing its exact bandwidth — useful for scanning unknown cells.
CellMapperCrowdsourced cell tower database
A community-built database of cell tower locations contributed by users running the CellMapper app. Records: operator (PLMN), RAT (LTE/5G/3G), band, Cell ID, eNB ID, PCI, EARFCN, and GPS location. Invaluable for: locating nearby towers, identifying which operator/band is on a specific mast, planning antenna direction, and understanding local network topology. 🔧 When troubleshooting your LTE antenna, CellMapper tells you which towers are nearby, their bands, and their direction — so you can point a directional antenna correctly.
LTE-Cell-ScannerSDR tool for passive LTE cell detection
Open-source software that uses an SDR receiver (RTL-SDR, HackRF, USRP) to passively scan for LTE cells. Detects PSS/SSS to find PCIs and EARFCNs, decodes the MIB to read bandwidth and frame number, and decodes SIB1 to extract PLMN and TAC. Fully passive — receive only, no SIM required, legal to use.
PRACHPhysical Random Access Channel
The channel a phone uses to initially access the network (Random Access Procedure). Uses Zadoff-Chu sequences (preambles) — 64 possible preambles, one selected randomly by the phone. Transmitted in specific time-frequency resources defined in SIB2. The base station responds with a Random Access Response, after which normal scheduled access begins.
IQ DataIn-phase and Quadrature baseband samples
The raw complex samples captured by an SDR receiver. I (in-phase) and Q (quadrature) are two orthogonal components that together represent the full amplitude and phase of a signal. Your HackRF captures IQ data. Software then processes this to demodulate signals, measure RSRP, detect cells, etc. Most SDR file formats (*.cf32, *.sc16) store IQ samples. 📡 Your RSA5065N also works with IQ data internally — it's what the spectrum analyser processes to produce the spectrum display you see.

Passive vs Active SDR on Cellular

All of the SDR techniques described here are receive-only (passive) — legal to use without any licence. You are not transmitting, not connecting to the network, and not decoding any user content (the data channels are encrypted). Reading broadcast channels (PBCH, SIB) is equivalent to reading publicly broadcast information. However, actively transmitting on cellular frequencies without appropriate authorisation is illegal — your Foundation licence will not cover this.

Quick Reference Card
The numbers you'll reach for most often

LTE Frame Quick Numbers

  • Frame = 10 ms
  • Subframe (TTI) = 1 ms
  • Slot = 0.5 ms
  • Subcarrier spacing = 15 kHz
  • Resource Block = 12 SC × 1 slot
  • 20 MHz channel = 100 RBs
  • Normal CP = 4.7 µs

RSRP at a Glance

  • ≥ −80 dBm → Excellent
  • −80 to −90 → Good
  • −90 to −100 → Fair
  • −100 to −110 → Poor
  • ≤ −110 dBm → Very poor / edge
  • RSRP ≈ RSSI − 10·log₁₀(12·N_RB)

UK MCC = 234

  • EE: 234-30
  • Vodafone: 234-15
  • O2: 234-10
  • Three: 234-20

LTE Cell ID Formula

  • Cell ID = eNB ID × 256 + Cell Index
  • 3-sector: Cell Index = 0, 1, 2
  • PCI range: 0–503
  • EARFCN → frequency: use online converter
