CHAPTER 01

Basic Antenna Theory

Figure 1: Antenna Schematic Symbol
Figure 2: Dipole Radiation Pattern

What is an Antenna?

An antenna is a transducer that converts electrical energy into electromagnetic waves (transmitting) or electromagnetic waves into electrical energy (receiving). It's the interface between guided waves on a transmission line and free-space radio waves.

💡
Key Principle — Reciprocity Theorem: Antenna characteristics (gain, pattern, impedance) are identical for transmit and receive operations.

Radiation Mechanism

Antennas work through the acceleration of electrons in a conductor:

  1. AC current flows through the antenna element
  2. Accelerating charges create changing electric and magnetic fields
  3. These fields detach from the conductor and propagate as electromagnetic waves
  4. Energy travels at the speed of light (c ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s)

Wavelength and Frequency Relationship

The fundamental equation linking frequency and physical antenna size:

λ = c / f

Where:
λ = wavelength (meters)
c = speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s)
f = frequency (Hz)

Practical Example:

  • 145 MHz (2 m band): λ = 300/145 = 2.07 metres
  • Half-wave dipole: λ/2 = 1.04 metres
  • Quarter-wave vertical: λ/4 = 0.52 metres

Essential Parameters

1. Radiation Pattern

The 3D distribution of radiated power as a function of direction. Typically shown as:

  • Azimuth pattern (horizontal plane)
  • Elevation pattern (vertical plane)
  • 3D pattern (complete visualisation)

Key Terms:

  • Main lobe: Direction of maximum radiation
  • Beamwidth: Angle between half-power (−3 dB) points
  • Side lobes: Undesired radiation in other directions
  • Front-to-back ratio: Power ratio between main lobe and opposite direction

2. Gain and Directivity

Gain (G): Ratio of power density in preferred direction vs. isotropic radiator. Measured in dBi (relative to isotropic) or dBd (relative to dipole). Conversion: dBi = dBd + 2.15

Reference Values:

  • Isotropic radiator: 0 dBi (theoretical)
  • Half-wave dipole: 2.15 dBi
  • 3-element Yagi: ~7–9 dBi
  • Large dish antenna: 30–50 dBi

3. Polarisation

The orientation of the electric field vector. Types:

  • Linear: Vertical or horizontal
  • Circular: Right-hand or left-hand (RHCP/LHCP)
  • Elliptical: Combination of linear and circular
⚡
Critical Note: Cross-polarised antennas (90° apart) have 20–30 dB isolation. Ionospheric propagation scrambles polarisation on HF.

4. Impedance and SWR

Feed-point Impedance (Z): Complex impedance at feed connection — typical values: 50 Ω, 75 Ω, 300 Ω, 450 Ω. Has resistive (R) and reactive (X) components: Z = R + jX

Standing Wave Ratio (SWR):

  • SWR = 1.0:1 — Perfect match (no reflections)
  • SWR < 1.5:1 — Excellent match (most applications)
  • SWR < 2.0:1 — Acceptable match (some power loss)
  • SWR > 3.0:1 — Poor match (significant losses, potential damage)

5. Bandwidth

The frequency range where antenna maintains acceptable performance:

  • Impedance bandwidth: Range where SWR < 2:1
  • Pattern bandwidth: Range where gain/pattern remain consistent

Factors Affecting Bandwidth: Element diameter (thicker = wider), antenna type (dipoles narrow, discones wide), Q-factor (lower Q = wider).

6. Resonance

Antenna is resonant when feed-point reactance = 0 (purely resistive). Occurs at specific frequencies, gives minimum SWR point and maximum radiation efficiency.

Common Resonant Lengths: Half-wave (λ/2) for dipoles; quarter-wave (λ/4) for verticals with ground plane; full-wave (1λ) for loop antennas.

1. Half-Wave Dipole

Type: Balanced, centre-fed  |  Bands: Any HF band (single-band when cut to length)  |  Length Formula: L (m) = 143 / f (MHz) × 0.95

ParameterValue
Gain2.15 dBi (reference standard)
Impedance73 Ω at resonance (centre-feed)
PatternFigure-8 (omnidirectional broadside)
PolarisationDepends on orientation (horizontal typical for HF)
✅
Advantages: Simple construction, inexpensive (wire and insulators), good efficiency, easy to match to 50 Ω or 75 Ω coax.
⚠️
Disadvantages: Single-band (unless using traps or tuner), requires supports at both ends, height affects radiation angle.

2. End-Fed Half-Wave (EFHW)

Type: Unbalanced, end-fed with matching transformer  |  Bands: Multi-band with appropriate transformer  |  Length: 0.95 × λ/2 for lowest frequency

ParameterValue
Impedance~2500–5000 Ω at high-Z end, transformed to 50 Ω
PatternSimilar to dipole
Matching49:1 transformer typically used
✅
Advantages: Only one support point needed, multi-band operation possible, no radials required, good for portable/stealth installations.

3. Vertical Antennas (Quarter-Wave)

Type: Ground plane, monopole  |  Bands: Any HF band  |  Length: λ/4 for vertical element

ParameterValue
Gain2–5 dBi (depending on ground system)
Impedance36 Ω (ideal ground), 25–50 Ω (practical)
PatternOmnidirectional in azimuth
Take-off AngleLow (good for DX)
Radials RequiredMinimum 4, optimal 16–120 (λ/4 each)

4. Magnetic Loop Antennas (Small)

Type: Tuned resonant loop  |  Bands: HF bands (remotely tuned)  |  Size: Circumference < λ/10 (typically 1–2 m diameter)

ParameterValue
Gain−5 to +1 dBi (compact size penalty)
Efficiency10–80% (depends on Q and losses)
BandwidthVery narrow (high Q)
PatternFigure-8 (null through loop centre)
✅
Advantages: Very compact (fits indoors), low noise pickup (magnetic coupling), directional null for QRM rejection, no ground system required.

5. Full-Wave Loop Antennas (Large)

Type: Resonant closed loop  |  Bands: Single or multi-band  |  Perimeter: 1λ at lowest frequency

Common Shapes: Square/Rectangle (easy to construct), Triangle/Delta (single support point), Circle (maximum gain, difficult to build).

ParameterValue
Gain3–4 dBi (1–2 dB over dipole)
Impedance100–120 Ω (fed in middle of side)
PatternOmnidirectional with some nulls off edges

1. Yagi-Uda Antenna

Type: Parasitic array, directional beam  |  Bands: VHF/UHF (50–1300 MHz typical)  |  Elements: 3–20+ (reflector, driver, directors)

ParameterValue
Gain7–20 dBi (depends on element count)
Front-to-Back15–30 dB
Beamwidth30–70° (narrows with more elements)
Impedance25–50 Ω (depends on design)

Design Parameters:

  • Reflector: 5% longer than driver
  • Directors: 5% shorter than driver, progressive shortening
  • Spacing: 0.1–0.25λ between elements
  • Boom length: Determines gain (longer = more gain)

2. J-Pole Antenna

Type: End-fed half-wave with matching stub  |  Bands: VHF/UHF (2 m, 70 cm popular)  |  Length: λ/2 radiating section + λ/4 matching stub

ParameterValue
Gain~2–3 dBi (comparable to dipole)
Impedance50 Ω (via matching stub)
PatternOmnidirectional with slight gain at horizon
PolarisationVertical

Variations: Slim Jim (folded dipole version), Super-J (two collinear half-waves), Collinear-J (optimised spacing for 3 dB gain).

3. Ground Plane Antenna

Type: Quarter-wave vertical with radials  |  Bands: VHF/UHF  |  Length: λ/4 vertical + 4 radials (λ/4 each)

ParameterValue
Gain2–5 dBi (depends on radial angle)
Impedance36 Ω (horizontal radials), 50 Ω (radials drooped 45°)
PatternOmnidirectional
Take-off AngleLow (~10–30°)

Radial Configurations: Horizontal → 36 Ω; 45° droop → 50 Ω (optimal match); 90° droop (vertical) → higher impedance.

4. Collinear Antennas

Type: Stacked in-phase half-wave elements  |  Bands: VHF/UHF  |  Elements: 2–8 half-wave sections

ParameterValue
Gain3–6 dBi (increases ~3 dB per doubling)
PatternOmnidirectional, compressed vertical beamwidth
Impedance50 Ω with proper phasing

Phasing Methods: Coil (separates elements, provides phase shift), Stub (half-wave phasing line), Folded (transmission line principles).

5. Discone Antenna

Type: Wideband omnidirectional  |  Frequency Range: Typically 100 MHz – 2 GHz (10:1 bandwidth or more)

ParameterValue
Gain2–4 dBi (slightly less than dipole)
Impedance50–75 Ω across wide range
PatternOmnidirectional
BandwidthMulti-octave coverage
💡
Perfect For Scanner Use: The discone's extremely wide bandwidth makes it ideal for monitoring multiple frequency bands with a single antenna.

1. Log-Periodic Dipole Array (LPDA)

Type: Wideband directional  |  Bands: Multi-octave (2:1 to 10:1)  |  Elements: Multiple dipoles of decreasing length

ParameterValue
Gain6–10 dBi (constant across bandwidth)
ImpedanceConstant (typically 50–200 Ω)
Beamwidth~60–80° (constant)
PatternUnidirectional

Applications: Multi-band HF operation, TV broadcast reception, wideband EMC testing, direction finding.

2. Cubical Quad

Type: Parasitic array using loops  |  Bands: HF through VHF  |  Elements: Full-wave loops (square typically)

ParameterValue
Gain1–2 dB more than Yagi (same boom)
Front-to-BackExcellent (25–35 dB)
Perimeter1λ (full wavelength)
Square SidesEach side = λ/4

3. Helical Antenna

Type: Axial-mode or normal-mode  |  Bands: VHF through microwave  |  Modes: Axial (endfire) or Normal (broadside)

ParameterValue (Axial Mode)
Circumference~1λ
Pitch0.25λ
Turns5–15
PolarisationCircular (RHCP or LHCP)
Gain12–20 dBi
Beamwidth30–60°

Applications: Satellite communications, GPS reception, ham radio satellite (LEO), radio astronomy.

4. Horn Antenna

Type: Waveguide aperture  |  Bands: Microwave (1–100 GHz)  |  Types: Pyramidal, conical, rectangular

ParameterValue
Gain10–25 dBi (depends on size)
PatternClean with low sidelobes
BandwidthWide (octave or more)

Applications: Feed horn for dishes, EMC testing (standard gain antenna), radio astronomy, radar systems.

5. Parabolic Dish Antenna

Type: Reflector with feed  |  Bands: Microwave (1–100 GHz)  |  Size: 0.5 m to 20 m+ diameter

ParameterValue
Gain20–60 dBi (depends on size)
Beamwidth1–10° (inversely proportional to diameter)
Efficiency50–70% (practical)

Feed Types: Prime Focus (feed at focal point, simple), Cassegrain (sub-reflector, compact), Offset (aperture unblocked, maximum efficiency).

Applications: Satellite communications, radio astronomy, EME (moonbounce), microwave links.

6. Beverage Antenna (Receiving Only)

Type: Long-wire traveling wave  |  Bands: LF through HF (160 m–40 m)  |  Length: 1–4 wavelengths (100–600 m typical)

ParameterValue
Gain−5 to +5 dBi (depends on length)
PatternUnidirectional (toward terminated end)
NoiseVery low (ground-coupled)
Height2–4 m above ground (relatively low)
💡
Extremely Low Noise: Beverage antennas provide exceptional noise performance for weak signal DX reception. Receive-only by design.

7. NVIS (Near Vertical Incidence Skywave)

Type: Low horizontal antennas  |  Bands: 40 m, 80 m, 160 m (3.5–7 MHz)  |  Height: λ/20 to λ/4 above ground

ParameterValue
PurposeRegional communications (0–400 km)
PatternHigh-angle radiation (70–90°)
ApplicationsEmergency comms, regional nets

8. Phased Arrays

Type: Multiple elements with controlled phase  |  Elements: 2–1000+ driven  |  Beam Steering: Electronic or mechanical

ParameterValue
PatternHighly controllable
GainSum of element gains + array factor
TypesBroadside, endfire, planar

Applications: Direction finding (DF), radar systems, amateur satellite tracking, interference rejection.

CHAPTER 05

Construction Tips & Guidelines

General Guidelines

1. Measure Twice, Cut Once

L = k × (300 / f_MHz)

Where:
k = 0.95 for wire dipoles
k = 0.94 for tubing
f_MHz = frequency in MHz

2. Use Quality Materials

  • Copper wire: 12–14 AWG minimum
  • Hardware: Stainless steel (corrosion resistant)
  • Insulators: UV-resistant materials
  • Coax: Quality cable (LMR-400, RG-213)

3. VNA is Essential

  • Measure before installation
  • Optimise at operating height
  • Document SWR across band
  • Save S11 plots for reference

4. Safety First

⚠ Critical Safety Considerations

Check for overhead power lines. Use proper support structures. Ground properly. Consider lightning protection. Never work alone on tall installations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

⚠️
1. Wrong Coax Length: Multiple of λ/2 can hide mismatch. Use random length or measure at antenna feedpoint.
⚠️
2. Poor Connections: Solder all connections, use quality connectors, weatherproof outdoor connections.
⚠️
3. Ignoring Environment: Nearby metal affects tuning, trees absorb RF (especially when wet), ground quality matters.
⚠️
4. Insufficient Testing: Test before installation, measure at operating height, check multiple frequencies across band.
