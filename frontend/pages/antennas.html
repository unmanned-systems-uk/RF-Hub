What is an Antenna?

An antenna is a transducer that converts electrical energy into electromagnetic waves (transmitting) or electromagnetic waves into electrical energy (receiving). It's the interface between guided waves on a transmission line and free-space radio waves.

Key Principle: Reciprocity Theorem

Antenna characteristics (gain, pattern, impedance) are identical for transmit and receive operations.

Radiation Mechanism

Antennas work through the acceleration of electrons in a conductor:

  1. AC current flows through the antenna element
  2. Accelerating charges create changing electric and magnetic fields
  3. These fields detach from the conductor and propagate as electromagnetic waves
  4. Energy travels at the speed of light (c ‚âà 3 √ó 10‚Å∏ m/s)

Essential Parameters

1. Radiation Pattern

The 3D distribution of radiated power as a function of direction. Typically shown as:

  • Azimuth pattern (horizontal plane)
  • Elevation pattern (vertical plane)
  • 3D pattern (complete visualization)

Key Terms:

  • Main lobe: Direction of maximum radiation
  • Beamwidth: Angle between half-power (-3dB) points
  • Side lobes: Undesired radiation in other directions
  • Front-to-back ratio: Power ratio between main lobe and opposite direction

2. Gain and Directivity

Gain (G): Ratio of power density in preferred direction vs. isotropic radiator

  • Measured in dBi (relative to isotropic) or dBd (relative to dipole)
  • Conversion: dBi = dBd + 2.15

Reference Values:

  • Isotropic radiator: 0 dBi (theoretical)
  • Half-wave dipole: 2.15 dBi
  • 3-element Yagi: ~7-9 dBi
  • Large dish antenna: 30-50 dBi

3. Polarization

The orientation of the electric field vector. Types:

  • Linear: Vertical or horizontal
  • Circular: Right-hand or left-hand (RHCP/LHCP)
  • Elliptical: Combination of linear and circular
Critical Note:

Cross-polarized antennas (90¬∞ apart) have 20-30 dB isolation. Ionospheric propagation scrambles polarization on HF.

4. Impedance and SWR

Feed-point Impedance (Z): Complex impedance at feed connection

  • Typical values: 50Œ©, 75Œ©, 300Œ©, 450Œ©
  • Has resistive (R) and reactive (X) components: Z = R + jX

Standing Wave Ratio (SWR): Measure of impedance match

  • SWR = 1.0:1 Perfect match (no reflections)
  • SWR < 1.5:1 Excellent match (most applications)
  • SWR < 2.0:1 Acceptable match (some power loss)
  • SWR > 3.0:1 Poor match (significant losses, potential damage)

5. Bandwidth

The frequency range where antenna maintains acceptable performance:

  • Impedance bandwidth: Range where SWR < 2:1
  • Pattern bandwidth: Range where gain/pattern remain consistent

Factors Affecting Bandwidth:

  • Element diameter: Thicker = wider bandwidth
  • Antenna type: Dipoles narrow, discones wide
  • Q-factor: Lower Q = wider bandwidth

6. Resonance

Antenna is resonant when feed-point reactance = 0 (purely resistive)

  • Occurs at specific frequencies
  • Minimum SWR point
  • Maximum radiation efficiency

Common Resonant Lengths:

  • Half-wave (Œª/2): Dipoles
  • Quarter-wave (Œª/4): Verticals with ground plane
  • Full-wave (1Œª): Loop antennas

General Guidelines

1. Measure Twice, Cut Once

L = k √ó (300 / f_MHz)

Where:
k = 0.95 for wire dipoles
k = 0.94 for tubing
f_MHz = frequency in MHz

2. Use Quality Materials

  • Copper wire: 12-14 AWG minimum
  • Hardware: Stainless steel (corrosion resistant)
  • Insulators: UV-resistant materials
  • Coax: Quality cable (LMR-400, RG-213)

3. VNA is Essential

  • Measure before installation
  • Optimize at operating height
  • Document SWR across band
  • Save S11 plots for reference

4. Safety First

  • Check for overhead power lines
  • Use proper support structures
  • Ground properly
  • Consider lightning protection
  • Never work alone on tall installations

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Wrong Coax Length

Multiple of Œª/2 can hide mismatch. Use random length or measure at antenna feedpoint.

2. Poor Connections

Solder all connections, use quality connectors, weatherproof outdoor connections.

3. Ignoring Environment

Nearby metal affects tuning, trees absorb RF (especially when wet), ground quality matters.

4. Insufficient Testing

Test before installation, measure at operating height, check multiple frequencies across band.
