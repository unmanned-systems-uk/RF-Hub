Understanding S11 Measurements

A practical guide to Return Loss, SWR, and Smith Chart interpretation on the Rigol RSA5065N

📡 Instrument: Rigol RSA5065N 📅 February 2026 ⏱ 15 min read
CHAPTER 01

What is S11?

S11 is one of the S-parameters (scattering parameters) used to describe how RF energy behaves when it encounters a device or component. Specifically, S11 measures the input reflection coefficient — the ratio of signal power reflected back from a device compared to the signal power that was sent into it.

In practical terms, when you connect an antenna to a VNA and measure S11, you are asking: "How much of the signal power that I send down this cable is being reflected back instead of being radiated by the antenna?"

A perfect antenna would absorb all the power sent to it and reflect nothing back — giving an S11 of negative infinity in dB. A perfect mirror (open circuit or short circuit) would reflect everything — giving an S11 of 0 dB. Real antennas fall somewhere in between.

💡
The S-Parameter Naming Convention: The numbers in "S11" tell you where the signal is measured and where it was injected. The first digit is the measurement port, the second is the stimulus port. So S11 means "signal goes into Port 1 and is measured coming back out of Port 1" — a reflection measurement. S21 would mean "signal goes into Port 1 and is measured at Port 2" — a transmission measurement.
CHAPTER 02

S11 Display Formats on the Rigol RSA5065N

The VNA captures S11 as a complex number (magnitude and phase) at each frequency point. This raw data can be displayed in several formats, each revealing different information about the device under test. On the Rigol, these are accessed via Trace > Trace Format.

Format What It Shows Best Used For
Log Mag Return loss in dB vs frequency Quantifying reflected power; comparing bands; pass/fail
SWR Standing Wave Ratio vs frequency Quick match assessment; familiar to amateur radio operators
Smith Chart Complex impedance (R + jX) on a circular plot Understanding mismatch nature; matching network design
Phase Phase angle of reflection vs frequency Cable length measurement; identifying resonant frequencies
Polar Magnitude and phase on a polar plot Similar to Smith chart but without impedance grid lines
Group Delay Rate of phase change vs frequency Identifying dispersion; filter characterisation

For antenna work, the three most important formats are Log Magnitude, SWR, and Smith Chart. These three views show the same underlying data in different ways, and each reveals information the others do not.

CHAPTER 03

Log Magnitude (Return Loss)

Log Magnitude displays S11 as return loss in decibels (dB) plotted against frequency. This is the most commonly used format for antenna measurements because it directly tells you how much power is being lost to reflections at each frequency.

Reading the Axes

Y-axis (Return Loss in dB): Always 0 dB or negative for S11. Zero dB means total reflection (all power bounced back). More negative values mean less reflection, which is better.

X-axis (Frequency): Spans the frequency range you configured. For narrow band measurements, this might cover a single LTE band. For wideband sweeps, it could cover hundreds of MHz.

What the Trace Shape Tells You

[ Figure 1: Log Magnitude traces — four antenna scenarios ]

Figure 1: Log Magnitude traces showing four different antenna scenarios — open circuit, poorly matched broadband, well-matched wideband, and narrowband resonant.

Flat line near 0 dB: Open circuit or no antenna connected. Almost all energy is reflected. This is what you should see after calibration with nothing connected to the port.

Flat line at moderate level: A broadband antenna that is consistently poorly matched. It radiates some energy but wastes a significant portion to reflections across the entire band.

Broad dip: A well-matched wideband antenna. The dip shows frequencies where the antenna resonates and the impedance is closest to 50Ω. The width of the dip indicates usable bandwidth.

Sharp narrow dip: A narrowband antenna. Excellent match at the resonant frequency but poor performance even slightly off-tune.

CHAPTER 04

Standing Wave Ratio (SWR)

SWR (also called VSWR — Voltage Standing Wave Ratio) is an alternative way to express the same impedance mismatch information shown by Log Magnitude. Where Log Mag works in decibels, SWR expresses the mismatch as a ratio from 1.0 to infinity. A perfect match is 1.0:1. An open or short circuit gives infinite SWR.

The Physical Meaning

When a signal travels down a transmission line and encounters an impedance mismatch, some energy reflects back. The forward-travelling and reflected waves combine to create a standing wave pattern on the cable. SWR is the ratio of the peak voltage to the minimum voltage in this standing wave pattern.

CHAPTER 05

The Mathematical Relationship

Return Loss (Log Mag) and SWR are mathematically linked through the reflection coefficient (Γ, gamma). They are not independent measurements — they are different representations of the same underlying quantity.

The Formulas

Reflection Coefficient (Γ): A complex number representing the ratio of reflected to incident voltage. Its magnitude ranges from 0 (perfect match) to 1 (total reflection).

Return Loss (dB) = 20 × log₁₀(|Γ|)

SWR = (1 + |Γ|) / (1 – |Γ|)

Power Reflected (%) = |Γ|² × 100
💡
Practical note: The relationship is non-linear. Small improvements in return loss near the -10 dB region produce significant improvements in SWR and reflected power. Beyond about -20 dB, further improvement has diminishing returns — you are already delivering over 99% of the power. This is why chasing a return loss better than -20 dB is rarely worthwhile for antenna installations.
CHAPTER 06

The Smith Chart

The Smith Chart is a specialised plot that shows complex impedance on a circular graph. While Log Mag and SWR only show how much signal is reflected (the magnitude), the Smith Chart also reveals the nature of the mismatch — whether the impedance is too high, too low, inductive, or capacitive.

[ Figure 4: Smith Chart with key reference points annotated ]

Figure 4: Smith Chart with key reference points — centre (50Ω perfect match), far right (open circuit), far left (short circuit), upper half (inductive), lower half (capacitive).

Key Landmarks

Centre — 50Ω

Purely resistive, perfect match. After calibration with the Load standard connected, you should see a dot right here. The closer your antenna measurement sits to the centre, the better.

Far Right — Open

Infinite impedance — an open circuit. This is where the trace should jump when you disconnect everything from the port after calibration.

Far Left — Short

Zero impedance — a short circuit. Must appear on the far left edge of the centre line. If it appears below (capacitive region), there is a calibration error.

SWR Circles

Dashed circles centred on the middle represent constant SWR values. A tight cluster of points near the centre means good match across the measured range.

⬆️
Upper half = Inductive (+jX): The antenna appears electrically too long, or there is excess inductance in the system.
⬇️
Lower half = Capacitive (−jX): The antenna appears electrically too short, or there is excess capacitance.
CHAPTER 07

What Cables Do to Your Measurements

When measuring an antenna through a length of coaxial cable, the cable affects your S11 measurement in two important ways.

Cable Loss Masks True Performance

The signal travels from the VNA up the cable to the antenna, reflects, and travels back down the cable. The cable attenuates the signal in both directions, so the reflected signal is weakened by twice the cable loss. This makes the return loss appear better than it really is — the cable is absorbing the reflected energy and hiding the true antenna mismatch.

⚠ Key Insight

When calibrating at the instrument port and measuring through a cable, the cable loss will make the antenna look better-matched than it actually is. For example, a cable with 1.5 dB loss each way adds 3 dB to the apparent return loss. An antenna with a true -8 dB return loss would appear to have -11 dB — crossing the threshold from "poor" to "acceptable" without any real improvement.

Calibration Sanity Check

With a valid calibration, disconnecting the antenna produces a dramatic change in the measurement — from a reasonable return loss to near 0 dB. Without valid calibration, the measurement barely changes whether the antenna is connected or not. If disconnecting the antenna does not significantly change your trace, the calibration is not working correctly.

CHAPTER 09

Which Display Format Should You Use?

If You Want To Know…Use This Format
"Is my antenna well-matched?"Log Mag — look for values below -10 dB
"What SWR will my radio see?"SWR — direct readout of the ratio
"Is the mismatch inductive or capacitive?"Smith Chart — check above or below centre line
"What impedance is the antenna presenting?"Smith Chart — read R + jX from the grid
"Over what bandwidth is the antenna usable?"Log Mag — measure the width of the dip below -10 dB
"Is my calibration valid?"Any format — disconnect the DUT and check for dramatic change
"Which LTE band gives the best performance?"Log Mag or SWR — compare per-band measurements
"Would an impedance matching network help?"Smith Chart — identify direction and magnitude of mismatch

The best approach is to use multiple formats together. Start with Log Mag or SWR for a quick pass/fail assessment, then switch to the Smith Chart to understand why any mismatch exists and what you might do about it.
